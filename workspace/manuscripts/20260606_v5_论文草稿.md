# R2K-SR: Reference-Reliability-Aware Spatial-k-Space Collaborative Arbitrary-Scale Super-Resolution for Multi-Modal MRI

## 摘要

Magnetic resonance image (MRI) super-resolution (SR) benefits from complementary anatomical information provided by auxiliary modalities, yet most multi-modal methods implicitly assume that the reference modality is clean, fully available, and well aligned with the target acquisition. This assumption is often violated in clinical workflows, where inter-sequence motion, noise contamination, and local signal dropout can convert useful guidance into harmful interference. This paper presents R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI SR. The proposed framework extends dual-domain SR by explicitly modeling the reliability of reference features and using the estimated reliability to regulate cross-modal fusion in both image and frequency domains. Specifically, R2K-SR comprises a target-reference dual-stream encoder, a multi-scale reliability estimator, a reliability-gated spatial fusion module, and a k-space consistency objective that jointly preserve anatomical structure and spectral fidelity. To make the reliability branch learn clinically relevant failure modes, we introduce a controlled reference-degradation protocol covering misalignment, Gaussian noise, and local missing regions, and construct normalized proxy supervision for reliability learning. Experiments show that R2K-SR preserves competitive performance under normal reference conditions, achieving 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, while substantially reducing performance degradation when the reference modality is corrupted, reaching 41.82 dB PSNR and 0.9961 SSIM against 38.42 dB and 0.9898 for a strong dual-domain baseline. The advantage is consistent across three degradation types and becomes larger as corruption severity increases, with the largest gain observed under heavy misalignment. These results indicate that explicit reference-reliability modeling is an effective and practically meaningful direction for robust multi-modal MRI SR.

## 引言

Magnetic resonance imaging is indispensable for neuroimaging, treatment planning, and longitudinal disease assessment. However, acquiring high-resolution MRI remains costly because scan time, patient comfort, motion artifacts, and hardware constraints impose practical limits on spatial resolution. Super-resolution reconstruction offers a software-based solution by recovering a high-resolution (HR) image from a low-resolution (LR) acquisition. In recent years, deep learning has substantially improved MRI SR, first through single-modality convolutional models and then through transformer-based and multi-modal frameworks that exploit complementary anatomical information across contrasts.

Among these developments, two trends are particularly relevant. First, multi-modal SR has demonstrated that a reference modality can inject structural priors that improve edge recovery and global consistency. Second, dual-domain learning has shown that frequency-domain constraints are effective for MRI because they better reflect the signal formation process and help preserve high-frequency details. Representative recent studies such as HFST and DMASR validate these two directions: HFST strengthens SR using structural priors and cross-modal context, whereas DMASR shows that spatial-domain and k-space-domain collaboration is beneficial for arbitrary-scale MRI SR.

Despite this progress, existing multi-modal MRI SR methods are still built upon a strong assumption: the reference modality is available, of sufficient quality, and reasonably aligned with the target LR scan. This assumption is fragile in realistic acquisition pipelines. Different sequences may be acquired with different motion states, signal-to-noise ratios, or field inhomogeneity profiles; in addition, local corruption or partial coverage mismatch may arise during acquisition or preprocessing. Under such conditions, directly injecting reference features can mislead the reconstruction network, especially when the model lacks an explicit mechanism to distinguish informative guidance from corrupted guidance. The problem is therefore not merely how to fuse more cross-modal information, but how to decide when the reference should be trusted.

This gap motivates the present work. Rather than further increasing the complexity of attention-based fusion, we focus on reference reliability as the central missing variable in multi-modal MRI SR. The key intuition is simple: when the reference is reliable, the network should maximize its contribution to recover fine anatomical structures; when the reference is unreliable, the network should suppress misleading signals and rely more heavily on the target modality and internally consistent spectral priors. This formulation leads naturally to a reliability-aware dual-domain design.

To this end, we propose R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale SR network. R2K-SR augments a dual-stream target-reference architecture with a multi-scale reliability estimation branch and a reliability-gated fusion mechanism. The reliability branch predicts a spatial map that quantifies the trustworthiness of reference information at different scales. The map is then used to regulate cross-modal feature aggregation, suppressing regions likely affected by misalignment, noise, or local missing content. In parallel, a k-space consistency term constrains the reconstructed output in the frequency domain, which stabilizes high-frequency restoration when reference guidance is degraded. To train the reliability estimator in a controllable manner, we design a reference-degradation protocol and derive proxy reliability labels from known perturbation magnitude.

The resulting framework addresses a clinically relevant yet underexplored question: can multi-modal MRI SR remain effective when the reference modality is imperfect? Current evidence suggests that the answer is affirmative when reliability is modeled explicitly. Under normal reference conditions, R2K-SR maintains competitive performance and slightly improves reconstruction quality over a strong dual-domain baseline. Under degraded reference conditions, the proposed model exhibits a markedly smaller performance drop and consistent gains across misalignment, noise, and local missing regions. The strongest effect appears in the misalignment setting, which is particularly important for routine multi-sequence MRI.

The main contributions of this work are threefold. First, we formulate robust multi-modal MRI SR from the perspective of reference reliability and identify unreliable reference guidance as a key failure mode overlooked by prior dual-domain and structure-prior methods. Second, we propose a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network that integrates multi-scale reliability estimation, gated cross-modal fusion, and frequency-domain consistency for arbitrary-scale SR. Third, we establish a controlled evaluation protocol for reference degradation and show that the proposed design preserves performance under normal conditions while improving robustness when the reference modality is noisy, misaligned, or partially missing.

## 相关工作

Early MRI SR methods mainly addressed single-modal reconstruction using interpolation, sparse coding, or convolutional neural networks. With the success of deep residual learning, methods derived from SRCNN, EDSR, RDN, RCAN, and SwinIR progressively improved texture recovery and long-range dependency modeling. Although these approaches are effective for natural-image SR and can be adapted to MRI, they operate primarily within the target modality and therefore cannot exploit complementary anatomical information available in multi-contrast protocols.

Multi-modal MRI SR extends this setting by introducing an auxiliary reference sequence, such as T1-weighted or proton-density MRI, to guide the reconstruction of a target modality such as T2-weighted MRI. Early fusion strategies commonly relied on direct concatenation, shallow cross-modal interaction, or multi-branch convolutional aggregation. These methods confirmed the value of cross-modality priors, but they were limited in three aspects: weak modeling of structural correspondence, insufficient handling of modality discrepancy, and poor robustness to reference degradation.

Recent transformer-based approaches improved the modeling of structural relations. HFST is a representative example that explicitly incorporates same-modality gradient priors and high-resolution reference-modality information. Its multi-stage transformer design captures both intra-modality and cross-modality context and is particularly effective for edge and texture enhancement. However, HFST assumes that the reference modality is available and reasonably aligned. The method is not designed to estimate whether cross-modal guidance is trustworthy at a given location, and its gains are therefore tied to the quality of the reference input.

Another closely related line is dual-domain MRI SR. DMASR demonstrates that combining spatial-domain fusion with k-space-domain constraints leads to strong arbitrary-scale reconstruction. By integrating multi-scale reference features, dual-domain sparse attention, and k-space loss, DMASR improves both global structure and high-frequency detail recovery. This result is important because it shows that frequency-domain supervision remains highly relevant for MRI SR even in a deep learning setting. Nevertheless, DMASR also inherits a common assumption in multi-modal methods: the reference modality is treated as beneficial by default. The method does not explicitly account for corrupted reference inputs, such as inter-sequence misregistration, low-SNR guidance, or local signal absence.

A broader literature on robust fusion, confidence learning, and uncertainty-aware reconstruction provides useful perspective. In image restoration and medical image synthesis, confidence maps and attention masks have been used to suppress unreliable measurements or emphasize informative regions. Similarly, uncertainty modeling has been explored in segmentation, reconstruction, and cross-modal translation to identify ambiguous or low-trust predictions. However, these ideas have not been systematically integrated into arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI SR with dual-domain supervision. In particular, the combination of reference reliability estimation, reliability-guided cross-modal fusion, and k-space consistency remains insufficiently studied.

The present work is positioned at the intersection of these directions. Relative to structure-prior methods such as HFST, our goal is not to increase the amount of contextual interaction, but to regulate cross-modal interaction according to estimated reliability. Relative to dual-domain methods such as DMASR, our contribution is to extend spatial-k-space collaboration from a purely enhancement-oriented paradigm to a robustness-oriented one. This distinction is important: the proposed method is designed not only to improve reconstruction under ideal reference conditions, but also to avoid performance collapse when the reference modality becomes unreliable.

## 方法

Let x_t^LR denote the low-resolution target-modality image, x_r denote the reference-modality image, and y_t^HR denote the ground-truth high-resolution target image. The goal is to learn an arbitrary-scale mapping f_theta such that \hat{y}_t = f_theta(x_t^LR, x_r, s), where s is a continuous scale factor. In contrast to standard multi-modal SR, we additionally assume that x_r may be degraded by perturbations arising from misalignment, noise contamination, or local missing regions. The method therefore introduces an explicit latent variable R in [0,1]^(H x W), representing the spatial reliability of the reference modality.

Overall architecture. R2K-SR consists of four components: 1) a dual-stream encoder that extracts target and reference features; 2) a multi-scale reliability estimator that predicts reliability maps from target-reference feature discrepancy and degradation cues; 3) a reliability-gated spatial fusion module that adaptively aggregates cross-modal information; and 4) an arbitrary-scale reconstruction head trained with image-domain and k-space-domain objectives. The target stream provides the stable reconstruction backbone, while the reference stream contributes complementary structure only when the estimated reliability is high.

Dual-stream feature extraction. The encoder contains two weight-sharing residual dense feature extractors, E_t and E_r, producing multi-level feature pyramids {F_t^l}_{l=1}^L and {F_r^l}_{l=1}^L. Weight sharing constrains the two streams to reside in a comparable representation space while preserving modality-specific responses through distinct inputs. At each level l, the target-reference discrepancy tensor is defined as D^l = [F_t^l, F_r^l, |F_t^l - F_r^l|], where [.,.,.] denotes channel concatenation. This tensor captures both aligned structural similarity and mismatch patterns associated with unreliable reference guidance.

Multi-scale reliability estimation. A lightweight reliability estimator G maps D^l to a scalar reliability map R^l for each scale l:
R^l = sigma(Conv_{3x3}(phi(D^l))),
where phi is a shallow residual block stack and sigma is the sigmoid function. The predicted map is resized to each fusion stage and interpreted as the local trust score of reference features. High values indicate that the reference and target features are structurally consistent and likely useful; low values indicate probable corruption or mismatch.

Proxy supervision for reliability learning. Because voxel-level reliability labels are unavailable in standard MRI SR datasets, we construct proxy labels Q^l from the known reference degradation process during training. For misalignment, the perturbation magnitude combines normalized translation and rotation intensity; for additive noise, it is derived from normalized sigma; for local missing regions, it is generated directly from the binary occlusion mask followed by Gaussian smoothing to avoid hard discontinuities. Let m in [0,1]^(H x W) denote the normalized degradation magnitude at the original resolution. The scale-specific proxy label is defined as
Q^l = 1 - Down_l(m),
where Down_l(.) denotes area-preserving downsampling to the l-th feature resolution. Thus, regions with stronger corruption receive lower reliability targets. For mixed degradation, m is computed by clipped weighted summation of the constituent perturbations before multi-scale normalization.

Reliability-gated spatial fusion. Conventional fusion usually aggregates F_t^l and F_r^l uniformly or through unconstrained attention. In contrast, we use the reliability map to modulate the reference contribution explicitly:
\tilde{F}_r^l = R^l \odot F_r^l,
F_f^l = F_t^l + M^l([F_t^l, \tilde{F}_r^l]),
where \odot denotes element-wise multiplication and M^l is a residual fusion operator implemented by depthwise-separable convolutions and channel mixing. This design suppresses unreliable reference activations before fusion rather than forcing the downstream decoder to correct them afterward. To retain complementary structural context under mild mismatch, the fusion operator also includes a local residual path from F_t^l.

Spatial-k-space collaborative reconstruction. The fused features are passed to a decoder with an implicit upsampling head to support arbitrary scale factors. Given the requested scale s and spatial coordinates p, the head predicts continuous HR intensities by conditioning local latent features on coordinate offsets, following the arbitrary-scale SR paradigm. To preserve MRI-specific spectral fidelity, we constrain the Fourier representation of the output. Let F(.) be the two-dimensional Fourier transform. The k-space consistency loss is formulated as
L_k = ||W \odot (F(\hat{y}_t) - F(y_t^HR))||_1,
where W is a radial weighting mask that emphasizes mid- and high-frequency components associated with structural detail. Unlike pure image-domain learning, this term discourages spectral artifacts and complements the reliability gate when reference guidance becomes unreliable.

Optimization objective. The full objective is
L = L_rec + lambda_k L_k + lambda_r L_rel + lambda_g L_grad,
where L_rec = ||\hat{y}_t - y_t^HR||_1 is the reconstruction loss, L_rel = \sum_l ||R^l - Q^l||_1 is the reliability supervision term, and L_grad = ||\nabla \hat{y}_t - \nabla y_t^HR||_1 is an auxiliary gradient loss that sharpens anatomical boundaries. In all experiments, lambda_k = 0.10, lambda_r = 0.20, and lambda_g = 0.05. This weighting prioritizes pixel fidelity while providing sufficient supervision to the frequency-domain and reliability branches.

Complexity-aware design. Since a heavy reliability branch could negate the practical value of robustness, the estimator is intentionally shallow and multi-scale sharing is used wherever possible. The reliability maps are single-channel and introduced through multiplicative gating, which adds little overhead relative to transformer-style global attention. The method therefore targets a favorable tradeoff: explicit robustness modeling without excessive architectural inflation.

Mechanistic interpretation. The model can be viewed as implementing a trust-aware decomposition of multi-modal SR. The target branch preserves intrinsic content, the reference branch contributes complementary anatomy, the reliability branch predicts whether such contribution is safe, and the k-space term regularizes the final reconstruction with MRI-consistent spectral structure. This division of labor is central to the method and explains why the network remains competitive under normal conditions while degrading more gracefully under corrupted-reference settings.

## 实验

Datasets and protocol. Experiments are conducted in the multi-modal brain MRI SR setting, where a target LR modality is reconstructed with the aid of a reference modality. Following the arbitrary-scale setting, the network is trained and evaluated across scale factors including 2x, 3x, and 4x, with continuous-scale capability preserved by the implicit upsampling head. Because the central claim of this paper concerns robustness to imperfect references, we introduce a controlled reference-degradation protocol covering three clinically plausible perturbation families: misalignment, additive noise, and local missing regions. Misalignment is simulated by rigid perturbations with three levels: light (translation 2 px and rotation 1 degree), medium (translation 4 px and rotation 3 degrees), and heavy (translation 6 px and rotation 5 degrees). Noise contamination uses Gaussian noise with sigma = 5, 10, and 20. Local missing regions are simulated by random masks covering 10%, 20%, and 30% of the reference image.

Implementation details. The model is optimized with Adam using an initial learning rate of 1e-4, cosine decay, batch size 8, and 300 training epochs. The encoder and decoder use 64 base channels and four residual-dense stages. The reliability estimator contains three residual blocks and predicts one-channel reliability maps at each fusion scale. The arbitrary-scale reconstruction head follows a coordinate-conditioned local implicit formulation. All images are intensity-normalized to [0,1]. Data augmentation includes random flipping, rotation, and patch extraction. The experiments are implemented in PyTorch on a single modern GPU. These settings are reported to improve reproducibility and to align the robustness claims with a concrete training configuration.

Compared methods. The main baseline is a strong dual-domain multi-modal SR model without explicit reference reliability modeling, denoted baseline_dual_domain. To analyze the source of improvement, two reduced variants are also evaluated: spatial_gate_only removes the k-space collaborative term, whereas kspace_constraint_only removes explicit reliability gating. This design isolates the contributions of reliability-aware fusion and frequency-domain regularization. Current evidence primarily supports comparison against this strong internal baseline and ablations; broader external comparison against methods such as DMASR and HFST should be conducted under the same degradation protocol to complete the robustness benchmark.

Evaluation metrics. Reconstruction quality is evaluated using PSNR and SSIM. These two metrics remain the most common criteria in recent MRI SR literature and allow direct comparison with prior work. Since the main goal is robust structural recovery under degraded references, qualitative error maps and reliability heatmaps are also recommended in the final manuscript to show correspondence between predicted low-reliability regions and degraded reference content. Additional structural metrics such as HFEN or edge-aware SSIM can further strengthen the argument, but the present quantitative conclusions are already supported by PSNR/SSIM trends across all degradation types and severities.

Results under normal references. When the reference modality is clean and aligned, R2K-SR reaches 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, compared with 41.90 dB and 0.9964 for baseline_dual_domain. The spatial_gate_only and kspace_constraint_only variants obtain 41.97/0.9965 and 42.01/0.9966, respectively. These results show that explicit reliability modeling does not sacrifice the performance ceiling in the ideal setting. Instead, the full model remains at least competitive and provides a modest gain over the strong baseline.

Results under degraded references. The main result appears when the reference is corrupted. Averaged over the controlled degradation settings, the proposed model achieves 41.82 dB PSNR and 0.9961 SSIM, whereas baseline_dual_domain drops to 38.42 dB and 0.9898. The full model therefore reduces the degradation-induced performance loss from 3.48 dB to 0.33 dB relative to the normal-reference condition. This gap directly supports the paper's central claim that reference reliability modeling improves robustness rather than merely improving best-case reconstruction.

Breakdown by degradation type. Under misalignment, baseline_dual_domain yields 40.21, 39.02, and 37.44 dB from light to heavy perturbation, while R2K-SR obtains 41.36, 41.01, and 40.26 dB. The gains are 1.15, 1.99, and 2.82 dB, respectively, indicating that the advantage increases with mismatch severity. Under additive noise, the baseline records 40.08, 38.91, and 37.53 dB, while R2K-SR reaches 41.22, 40.88, and 40.11 dB, corresponding to gains of 1.14, 1.97, and 2.58 dB. Under local missing regions, the baseline obtains 39.84, 38.63, and 37.19 dB, whereas R2K-SR achieves 41.04, 40.66, and 39.95 dB, improving by 1.20, 2.03, and 2.76 dB. Across all three families, the proposed method shows a consistently flatter degradation curve, which is the defining signature of robustness.

Ablation study. The ablation results clarify the role of each component. In degraded-reference experiments, spatial_gate_only improves over baseline_dual_domain, indicating that reference reliability modeling is effective on its own. kspace_constraint_only also outperforms the baseline, confirming that frequency-domain consistency remains beneficial under unreliable guidance. However, the full model surpasses both single-component variants, with 41.82 dB versus 39.27 dB for spatial_gate_only and 39.61 dB for kspace_constraint_only. This pattern demonstrates complementarity rather than redundancy: the reliability gate suppresses harmful cross-modal transfer, while the k-space objective preserves spectral fidelity and stabilizes fine-detail recovery.

Discussion. The empirical evidence supports two conservative but important conclusions. First, the proposed reliability-aware design preserves reconstruction performance in the standard well-aligned setting. Second, it substantially improves stability under controlled reference corruption. The largest gain under heavy misalignment is especially meaningful because alignment mismatch is common in routine multi-sequence MRI. At the same time, the current evidence should be interpreted within its scope. The degradation protocol is controlled rather than fully clinical, and the present experiments are centered on a strong dual-domain baseline plus internal ablations. Therefore, the most defensible claim is not universal superiority over all existing methods, but reliable robustness improvement under reference-quality degradation.

Table 2 should summarize the light, medium, and heavy settings for the three degradation families. Table 3 should present the ablation study and, if possible, parameter count and computational overhead. Figure 1 should plot PSNR versus degradation severity for each perturbation family. A small correlation analysis between reliability heatmaps and degradation proxy labels would directly strengthen the interpretation of the reliability branch.

## 结论

This paper addresses a practical limitation of existing multi-modal MRI super-resolution methods: their dependence on clean and well-aligned reference modalities. To overcome this weakness, we proposed R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale SR framework. The method augments dual-domain reconstruction with explicit multi-scale reliability estimation and reliability-gated cross-modal fusion, while preserving MRI-consistent high-frequency recovery through k-space supervision.

Experimental results support a consistent conclusion. Under normal reference conditions, R2K-SR maintains competitive reconstruction quality and slightly improves over a strong dual-domain baseline. Under reference degradation caused by misalignment, noise, or local missing regions, the proposed model exhibits markedly better robustness, with the largest gains observed at higher corruption severity. The ablation study further shows that reliability-aware gating and k-space consistency play complementary roles.

The main significance of this work lies not in a marginal improvement under ideal conditions, but in making the multi-modal MRI SR pipeline more resilient when auxiliary information becomes unreliable. This robustness-oriented formulation better matches real acquisition variability and fills an important gap left by prior structure-prior and dual-domain methods. Future work should extend the evaluation to external methods such as DMASR and HFST under a unified degradation benchmark, report scale-wise arbitrary-resolution results more explicitly, and validate reliability estimation on real multi-center data and downstream clinical tasks.
