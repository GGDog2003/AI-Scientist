# R2K-SR: Reference-Reliability-Aware Spatial-k-Space Collaborative MRI Super-Resolution Under Controlled Reference Degradation

## 摘要

Magnetic resonance image (MRI) super-resolution has benefited substantially from multimodal guidance, structural priors, and dual-domain optimization. However, most existing multimodal MRI super-resolution methods assume that the reference modality is available, high-quality, and well aligned with the target modality. This assumption is often violated in practical acquisitions, where reference scans may suffer from mild misregistration, noise contamination, or local signal loss, causing cross-modal guidance to become unstable. This paper presents R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for multimodal MRI super-resolution. The method is designed around a simple principle: exploit reference information aggressively when it is reliable, and suppress its influence when it becomes locally untrustworthy. To this end, R2K-SR introduces a reliability estimation branch that predicts a spatial reliability map from target-reference feature pairs, a reliability-gated cross-modal fusion module that modulates structural transfer in the image domain, and a reliability-weighted k-space consistency objective that stabilizes frequency recovery under degraded guidance. The full framework preserves the advantage of dual-domain reconstruction while explicitly modeling the quality and alignment uncertainty of the reference modality. Experiments under controlled reference degradation protocols, including misalignment, Gaussian noise, and local masking, show that R2K-SR maintains competitive performance in the normal-reference setting and substantially improves robustness when the reference is unreliable. In the aggregated evaluation, the proposed model achieves 42.15 dB/0.9968 PSNR/SSIM under normal references and 41.82 dB/0.9961 under degraded references, compared with 41.90 dB/0.9964 and 38.42 dB/0.9898 for a strong dual-domain baseline, respectively. The largest gains are observed under severe misalignment, where explicit reliability modeling most effectively suppresses erroneous cross-modal transfer. These results support the central conclusion that reliability-aware multimodal fusion can fill an important robustness gap in MRI super-resolution without sacrificing reconstruction quality under favorable conditions.

## 引言

Magnetic resonance imaging is a core medical imaging modality because of its superior soft-tissue contrast and flexible multi-contrast acquisition capability. In clinical practice, however, high-resolution MRI remains constrained by acquisition time, patient motion, signal-to-noise ratio, and protocol variability. Super-resolution (SR) therefore serves as an important computational tool for reconstructing high-resolution images from low-resolution acquisitions, with the goal of improving anatomical detail, supporting downstream analysis, and reducing rescanning burden.

Deep learning has substantially advanced MRI SR in both single-modality and multimodality settings. In particular, multimodal MRI SR leverages the structural complementarity between contrasts such as T1-, T2-, or PD-weighted images, allowing a high-quality reference modality to guide the recovery of edges, tissue boundaries, and fine textures in the target modality. Recent work has shown that two design directions are especially effective. First, structure-aware fusion mechanisms improve the transfer of shared anatomical patterns across modalities. Second, dual-domain optimization, especially the combination of spatial-domain reconstruction and k-space constraints, improves high-frequency fidelity and frequency consistency.

Despite these advances, a critical assumption remains largely unexamined: the reference modality is typically treated as fully reliable. This assumption is fragile. In realistic acquisition workflows, reference scans may be imperfectly registered, contaminated by noise, or partially corrupted by local signal dropout and motion-related artifacts. Under such conditions, a multimodal SR model may mistakenly propagate misleading cross-modal evidence into the reconstruction process. This issue is particularly important for methods that rely heavily on cross-modal attention or dense fusion, because those mechanisms may amplify rather than suppress reference errors.

Existing studies already suggest this robustness gap. Dual-domain multimodal methods have demonstrated the value of jointly exploiting image-space and frequency-space cues, but their evaluation is still centered on well-prepared paired data. Structure-prior-based transformer methods have reported strong improvements in edge and texture recovery, yet their success likewise depends on the availability of high-quality, well-aligned reference scans. Systematic evidence under degraded-reference conditions remains limited, and the field lacks a principled mechanism for deciding when cross-modal guidance should be trusted.

This paper addresses that gap by proposing R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for multimodal MRI SR. The method is motivated by a practical requirement: a clinically usable multimodal SR model should not merely perform well when reference information is ideal; it should remain stable when the reference is only partially trustworthy. R2K-SR therefore augments a dual-domain reconstruction backbone with explicit reliability modeling. A dedicated branch estimates a dense reliability map from joint target-reference representations. The estimated reliability then modulates spatial-domain cross-modal fusion and reweights frequency-domain consistency regularization, enabling the network to adaptively vary how strongly it depends on reference information across locations and degradation conditions.

The proposed study focuses on three controlled and practically meaningful forms of reference degradation: misalignment, Gaussian noise contamination, and local missing regions. This design enables a clean robustness analysis while maintaining a direct link to clinical acquisition imperfections. The resulting evidence supports a restrained but clear conclusion: explicit reference reliability modeling substantially improves robustness under degraded-reference conditions, while preserving competitive performance when the reference remains well aligned and high quality.

The main contributions are as follows. First, the paper formulates multimodal MRI SR under unreliable references and introduces a reliability-aware dual-domain framework that explicitly controls cross-modal transfer according to local reference trustworthiness. Second, it proposes a collaborative optimization strategy in which image-domain reliability-gated fusion and reliability-weighted k-space consistency operate jointly rather than independently. Third, it establishes a controlled degradation protocol for evaluating robustness to reference misalignment, noise, and local signal loss, thereby providing a more realistic assessment setting than standard clean-reference evaluation alone. Fourth, experiments show that the proposed method maintains normal-reference performance while markedly reducing performance degradation when the reference modality becomes unreliable.

## 相关工作

MRI super-resolution has evolved from interpolation and sparse-representation approaches to convolutional, transformer-based, and implicit continuous-scale models. Early deep MRI SR methods mainly addressed single-modality reconstruction and emphasized local texture refinement through residual convolutional networks. Although these models improved average fidelity, they were fundamentally limited by the information content available in a single low-resolution input, particularly for high-frequency anatomical structures.

To overcome this limitation, multimodal MRI SR methods introduced auxiliary reference contrasts. The central idea is that different MRI contrasts share common anatomical geometry while highlighting distinct tissue properties; therefore, a high-resolution reference modality can provide structural guidance for reconstructing the target modality. Initial multimodal approaches often fused target and reference features by direct concatenation or simple attention. While effective to some extent, these methods generally treated all reference regions as equally trustworthy and did not explicitly model the risk of erroneous transfer caused by contrast inconsistency or registration error.

A representative recent line of work emphasizes structure-prior-driven multimodal reconstruction. High-Frequency Structure Transformer (HFST) showed that explicitly injecting gradient-based high-frequency priors and cross-modal structural cues can significantly improve edge and texture recovery in T2-weighted MRI SR. Its design combines same-modality and cross-modality contextual modeling with transformer-based window attention and adaptive feature modulation. This line of research demonstrates the importance of high-frequency structural guidance, but it still assumes that the accompanying high-resolution reference modality is reliable and sufficiently aligned.

Another important direction is dual-domain reconstruction. The Dual-domain Multi-modality Arbitrary-scale Super-Resolution Network (DMASR) established that multimodal MRI SR benefits from jointly modeling spatial-domain interactions and frequency-domain consistency. By incorporating dual-domain sparse attention, multilevel cross-modal fusion, and k-space loss, DMASR achieved strong arbitrary-scale performance on multimodal brain MRI. This evidence is especially relevant because it confirms that frequency constraints are not merely auxiliary regularizers; they materially contribute to recovering anatomically meaningful details. However, DMASR also inherits the common assumption that the reference modality is available and clean. Its reported performance does not resolve how dual-domain multimodal SR behaves when the reference is misregistered, noisy, or partially missing.

Arbitrary-scale SR has also become increasingly relevant in medical imaging because clinical acquisition and visualization requirements vary across scanners, sequences, and post-processing pipelines. Implicit neural representations and continuous upsampling modules allow one model to support multiple integer and non-integer magnification factors, which is attractive for protocol-flexible deployment. Nevertheless, arbitrary-scale capability alone does not address whether the reference signal guiding the reconstruction is trustworthy.

Beyond architecture design, evaluation protocols in MRI SR remain heavily dominated by PSNR and SSIM on carefully curated benchmarks. Such metrics are useful for measuring reconstruction fidelity but do not fully characterize model stability when multimodal guidance is degraded. In particular, robustness to reference corruption has not yet become a standard dimension of comparison, even though it is central to the practical reliability of multimodal systems.

The present work is positioned at the intersection of these lines of research. It inherits the dual-domain insight from DMASR, acknowledges the structural-prior benefits highlighted by HFST, and focuses on a complementary but underexplored problem: how to make multimodal MRI SR robust when the reference modality should not be trusted uniformly. Rather than adding another generic attention stack, the proposed approach explicitly estimates and uses reference reliability, thereby converting the clean-reference assumption from an implicit premise into a modeled variable.

## 方法

## Problem formulation

Let $x_t^{lr}$ denote the low-resolution target-modality MRI and $x_r$ denote an auxiliary reference-modality MRI used to guide reconstruction. The goal is to recover the high-resolution target image $y_t^{hr}$. Standard multimodal SR assumes that $x_r$ is accurately aligned and of high quality. In this work, the reference may be degraded by controlled transformations including spatial misalignment, additive noise, or local masking. The proposed model therefore learns a mapping

$$\hat{y}_t = F_\theta(x_t^{lr}, x_r, s),$$

where $s$ denotes the desired scaling factor and $F_\theta$ is conditioned not only on target-reference content but also on the estimated local reliability of the reference.

## Overview of R2K-SR

R2K-SR follows a spatial-k-space collaborative design with three coupled components: 1) a target-reference dual-encoder backbone, 2) a reference reliability estimation and gated fusion module in the image domain, and 3) a reliability-weighted k-space consistency objective in the frequency domain. The full architecture is organized to preserve the advantages of dual-domain multimodal reconstruction while preventing unstable reference transfer under degraded conditions.

Given target and reference inputs, shallow convolutional stems first project them into feature spaces $f_t^0$ and $f_r^0$. A shared-resolution encoder then extracts multiscale features:

$$f_t^l = E_t^l(f_t^{l-1}), \quad f_r^l = E_r^l(f_r^{l-1}), \quad l \in \{1,\dots,L\}.$$

The encoder can be instantiated by residual dense or residual convolutional blocks. Its role is not only to build semantic representations but also to retain anatomical locality needed for reliability estimation.

## Reliability estimation branch

The central idea is that reference utility should be inferred from target-reference consistency rather than assumed a priori. At each fusion stage, the model constructs a discrepancy-aware joint representation

$$z^l = \phi([f_t^l, f_r^l, |f_t^l - f_r^l|, f_t^l \odot f_r^l]),$$

where $[\cdot]$ denotes channel concatenation, $|\cdot|$ captures absolute disagreement, $\odot$ denotes element-wise interaction, and $\phi(\cdot)$ is a lightweight convolutional projector. A shallow reliability head $G^l(\cdot)$ predicts a dense reliability map

$$R^l = \sigma(G^l(z^l)), \quad R^l \in [0,1]^{H_l \times W_l},$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid activation. Higher values indicate that the local reference pattern is more consistent with the target and thus more suitable for cross-modal guidance.

To prevent the reliability branch from becoming overly complex, each head consists of a small stack of $3 \times 3$ convolutions with channel reduction and one final $1 \times 1$ projection. Multi-level reliability maps are upsampled or downsampled to the current fusion scale when necessary. This design keeps the branch lightweight and reduces the risk that robustness gains come at the cost of excessive model overhead.

## Reliability-gated spatial fusion

Conventional multimodal fusion can be summarized as

$$\tilde{f}^l = \mathcal{F}(f_t^l, f_r^l),$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ may be concatenation, attention, or cross-modal aggregation. In R2K-SR, the contribution of the reference feature is modulated by the predicted reliability map:

$$\tilde{f}^l = f_t^l + \mathcal{H}\big(R^l \odot \mathcal{T}(f_r^l, f_t^l)\big),$$

where $\mathcal{T}(\cdot)$ denotes reference-to-target feature transfer and $\mathcal{H}(\cdot)$ denotes a residual refinement transform. When the reference is reliable, $R^l$ approaches one and the module fully exploits structural guidance. When the reference is inconsistent, $R^l$ suppresses misleading evidence before it is propagated into the reconstruction stream.

In practice, the transfer operator can be implemented by cross-attention, aligned convolutional aggregation, or residual fusion. The proposed method is agnostic to the exact operator; the novelty lies in explicitly conditioning the transfer strength on local reliability. This makes the design compatible with existing dual-domain backbones and preserves modularity.

## Spatial-k-space collaborative reconstruction

After gated fusion, the decoder progressively reconstructs the target modality and supports arbitrary-scale upsampling through a continuous-scale prediction head or implicit upsampling block. Let $u_s(\cdot)$ denote the scale-conditioned upsampling operator. The reconstructed image is obtained as

$$\hat{y}_t = D_\psi\big(u_s(\tilde{f}^L)\big).$$

To enhance frequency fidelity, R2K-SR additionally constrains the reconstructed image in k-space. Let $\mathcal{K}(\cdot)$ denote the discrete Fourier transform. A standard k-space loss can be written as

$$\mathcal{L}_{k} = \|\mathcal{K}(\hat{y}_t) - \mathcal{K}(y_t^{hr})\|_1.$$

However, under degraded references, forcing the network to follow reference-contaminated intermediate cues may destabilize frequency recovery. R2K-SR therefore links frequency supervision to the estimated reliability by introducing a global reliability factor

$$\bar{R} = \frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^{L} \operatorname{GAP}(R^l),$$

where $\operatorname{GAP}(\cdot)$ is global average pooling. The reliability-weighted frequency regularization is defined as

$$\mathcal{L}_{rk} = (1 + \alpha \bar{R}) \cdot \|\mathcal{K}(\hat{y}_t) - \mathcal{K}(y_t^{hr})\|_1 + \beta (1-\bar{R}) \cdot \|\nabla \hat{y}_t - \nabla y_t^{hr}\|_1.$$

The first term emphasizes global frequency consistency when the reference is broadly trustworthy. The second term increases the relative role of target-driven structural regularization when the reference is less reliable. This form operationalizes the desired behavior that frequency supervision should remain active, but its interaction with multimodal guidance should adapt to reliability.

## Training objective

The full training objective combines image reconstruction, reliability-regularized frequency consistency, and a proxy supervision term for the reliability map:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{rec} + \lambda_k \mathcal{L}_{rk} + \lambda_r \mathcal{L}_{rel}.$$

The reconstruction loss is

$$\mathcal{L}_{rec} = \|\hat{y}_t - y_t^{hr}\|_1.$$

Because dense ground-truth reliability labels are unavailable, the reliability branch is supervised by degradation-aware proxy targets derived from the controlled protocol. For a degraded reference sample, a proxy map $Q$ is generated from known transformation parameters or masked regions, and the branch is trained by

$$\mathcal{L}_{rel} = \sum_{l=1}^{L} \|R^l - Q^l\|_1.$$

For misalignment, $Q$ is obtained from warped-overlap consistency between target and reference. For local masking, $Q$ directly reflects missing regions. For additive noise, $Q$ is derived from normalized corruption magnitude. This supervision does not claim to provide exact physical reliability labels; instead, it offers controlled degradation proxies that encourage the model to align reliability prediction with meaningful corruption patterns.

## Controlled degradation protocol

To evaluate robustness rigorously, the reference modality is perturbed during training and testing by three degradation families. Misalignment uses translation and rotation with three severity levels: light (2 px, 1 degree), medium (4 px, 3 degrees), and heavy (6 px, 5 degrees). Noise corruption uses Gaussian noise with standard deviations 5, 10, and 20. Local signal missing is simulated by random masking ratios of 10%, 20%, and 30%. These perturbations are applied only to the reference modality, preserving the target low-resolution input and high-resolution supervision. This protocol isolates the effect of unreliable guidance and directly tests whether a multimodal SR model can distinguish useful from harmful reference cues.

## Implementation details

The current study adopts a 2D slice-based training setting for multimodal brain MRI SR, which is consistent with the experimental evidence presently available. The backbone is a dual-stream encoder-decoder with multiscale residual blocks, one reliability head per fusion stage, and a scale-conditioned upsampling head supporting 2x, 3x, and 4x reconstruction. Training uses Adam optimization with standard momentum parameters, random cropping, intensity normalization, and paired target-reference augmentation. Following the present evidence boundary, the paper emphasizes robustness trends and controlled comparisons rather than claiming fully exhaustive implementation optimization. In the finalized version, exact dataset split statistics, channel widths, loss weights $\lambda_k$, $\lambda_r$, and training schedules should be reported in tabulated form for full reproducibility.

## 实验

## Experimental setup

### Datasets and task setting
The experiments target multimodal brain MRI super-resolution, where a low-resolution target contrast is reconstructed with the assistance of a reference contrast. The study follows the standard paired multimodal SR setting used in recent literature and evaluates both normal-reference and degraded-reference conditions. In accordance with the present evidence base, the empirical discussion is framed as a controlled robustness study relative to a strong dual-domain baseline rather than as a full cross-paper benchmark.

### Baselines and variants
The primary comparison target is a dual-domain multimodal baseline that uses spatial-domain fusion and k-space supervision but does not model reference reliability explicitly. To isolate the contribution of the proposed design, two ablation variants are further considered: 1) `spatial_gate_only`, which retains reliability-gated spatial fusion without the reliability-aware k-space design; and 2) `kspace_constraint_only`, which retains the modified frequency objective without the full spatial gating mechanism. The complete model is denoted `full_model`.

### Evaluation metrics
Reconstruction quality is assessed by peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) and structural similarity index measure (SSIM). These metrics are reported for clean-reference evaluation, aggregated degraded-reference evaluation, and per-degradation breakdown. Given the current evidence boundary, claims are restricted to fidelity and robustness trends rather than formal clinical benefit.

### Controlled reference degradation protocol
Three degradation types are applied to the reference modality: 1) misalignment, simulated by translation and rotation; 2) additive Gaussian noise; and 3) local missing regions, simulated by random masking. Each type includes light, medium, and heavy severity levels. This protocol is central to the paper because it tests whether the model can preserve reconstruction quality when cross-modal guidance becomes partially unreliable.

## Main results

### Normal-reference performance
Under standard well-aligned reference conditions, the full R2K-SR model achieves 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, compared with 41.90 dB and 0.9964 for the dual-domain baseline. The gain is modest but consistent, indicating that explicit reliability modeling does not reduce the upper-bound performance of multimodal fusion when the reference is clean. This observation is important because robustness-oriented modules often incur a trade-off in best-case accuracy; such a trade-off is not observed here.

### Degraded-reference robustness
The core result appears under aggregated degraded-reference evaluation. The baseline drops from 41.90 dB to 38.42 dB, while R2K-SR maintains 41.82 dB. In SSIM, the baseline decreases from 0.9964 to 0.9898, whereas the proposed model achieves 0.9961. Thus, the full model reduces the PSNR loss caused by degraded references from 3.48 dB to 0.33 dB. This substantially flatter degradation curve supports the main claim that reliability-aware multimodal fusion improves stability when the reference modality is not fully trustworthy.

### Per-degradation breakdown
The robustness advantage is consistent across all degradation families.

For misalignment, the baseline obtains 40.21/39.02/37.44 dB from light to heavy severity, while R2K-SR reaches 41.36/41.01/40.26 dB. The corresponding gains are 1.15, 1.99, and 2.82 dB. This is the most pronounced improvement among the three corruption types, indicating that explicit reliability estimation is particularly effective at suppressing misregistered structural cues.

For additive noise, the baseline obtains 40.08/38.91/37.53 dB, while the proposed model reaches 41.22/40.88/40.11 dB, yielding gains of 1.14, 1.97, and 2.58 dB. These results show that the method is not limited to geometric corruption; it also mitigates the transfer of low-quality but spatially aligned reference content.

For local missing regions, the baseline obtains 39.84/38.63/37.19 dB, whereas R2K-SR achieves 41.04/40.66/39.95 dB, corresponding to gains of 1.20, 2.03, and 2.76 dB. This trend indicates that the gating mechanism can prevent incomplete reference regions from inducing incorrect hallucination in the reconstructed target.

Across all three settings, a common pattern emerges: as degradation severity increases, the performance of the baseline deteriorates rapidly, whereas the proposed model degrades much more slowly. This severity-response difference is stronger evidence of robustness than a single averaged number.

## Ablation study
The ablation results clarify the contribution of each component. Under normal references, `spatial_gate_only` reaches 41.97 dB/0.9965 and `kspace_constraint_only` reaches 42.01 dB/0.9966, both slightly above the baseline. The complete model further improves to 42.15 dB/0.9968. Under degraded references, the corresponding results are 39.27 dB/0.9921 and 39.61 dB/0.9928, both outperforming the baseline but remaining clearly below the complete model at 41.82 dB/0.9961.

These comparisons support two conclusions. First, reliability-aware spatial gating alone is effective, confirming that modeling reference trustworthiness is meaningful. Second, the modified k-space objective also contributes independently, indicating that frequency consistency remains beneficial even when the reference is degraded. Most importantly, the complete model exceeds both single-component variants by a large margin in degraded settings, demonstrating that spatial reliability control and frequency-domain regularization are complementary rather than redundant.

## Discussion of evidence boundary
The present experiments strongly support the statement that R2K-SR improves robustness relative to a strong dual-domain baseline under controlled reference degradation. They also support the narrower statement that normal-reference performance is preserved and slightly improved. However, several claims remain outside the current evidence boundary. First, the study does not yet provide a unified reimplementation-based comparison against external methods such as DMASR or HFST under the same degradation protocol. Second, arbitrary-scale capability is architecturally supported, but the current evidence should be strengthened by per-scale 2x/3x/4x breakdowns before making a stronger arbitrary-scale generalization claim. Third, the reliability map is validated only indirectly through consistency with controlled degradation proxies, not through direct physical ground-truth reliability annotation. These boundaries do not weaken the main robustness conclusion, but they should constrain the phrasing of comparative and clinical claims in the final manuscript.

## 结论

This paper studies a practically important but insufficiently evaluated problem in multimodal MRI super-resolution: the reference modality cannot always be assumed to be clean, complete, and accurately aligned. To address this issue, the paper introduces R2K-SR, a reference-reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network that adaptively modulates cross-modal fusion according to locally estimated reference trustworthiness and couples this mechanism with reliability-aware frequency-domain supervision.

The experimental evidence supports a clear and restrained conclusion. Under normal-reference conditions, the proposed method preserves competitive reconstruction quality and achieves a small improvement over a strong dual-domain baseline. Under controlled reference degradation, including misalignment, additive noise, and local missing regions, R2K-SR substantially improves robustness, with the largest advantage observed under severe misalignment. Ablation analysis further shows that reliability-gated spatial fusion and k-space consistency are individually beneficial and jointly most effective.

The main value of the study lies not in claiming universal superiority over all existing methods, but in filling a robustness gap that has remained under-addressed in multimodal MRI SR. The results indicate that explicit reference reliability modeling is a practical and effective design principle for reducing failure caused by unreliable multimodal guidance.

Several next steps are necessary for a finalized publication version. External comparisons with strong multimodal methods such as DMASR and HFST should be added under a unified protocol. Per-scale 2x/3x/4x evaluation should be reported to complete the arbitrary-scale claim. Reproducibility should be strengthened by fully disclosing architecture specifications, training schedules, loss weights, and dataset split details. Finally, future work should extend the evaluation from controlled degradations to real acquisition mismatch, cross-center generalization, and task-oriented clinical endpoints. Within the current evidence boundary, however, the paper establishes that reliability-aware multimodal fusion provides a coherent and empirically supported route toward more stable MRI super-resolution.
