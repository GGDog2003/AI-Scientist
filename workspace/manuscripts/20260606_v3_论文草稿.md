# R2K-SR: Reliability-Aware Spatial-k-Space Collaborative Arbitrary-Scale Super-Resolution for Multi-Modal MRI Under Controlled Reference Degradation

## 摘要

Magnetic resonance image (MRI) super-resolution (SR) has recently benefited from multi-modal reference guidance, spatial-frequency dual-domain modeling, and arbitrary-scale reconstruction. However, most existing methods implicitly assume that the reference modality is high-quality and well registered. This assumption is often violated in practice due to inter-sequence misalignment, noise contamination, or local signal corruption, causing reference information to change from a useful prior into a source of structured interference. To address this issue, this paper presents R2K-SR, a reference reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale SR network for multi-modal MRI. The proposed framework introduces an explicit reliability estimation branch that predicts a spatial reliability map from the target low-resolution modality and the reference modality, and uses this map to modulate cross-modal fusion in both image space and frequency space. A reliability-gated spatial fusion module suppresses unreliable reference transfer at ambiguous locations, while a reliability-weighted k-space consistency objective preserves frequency fidelity without over-penalizing degraded reference regions. The network further adopts continuous-scale coordinate conditioning to support arbitrary magnification factors within a prescribed range. Experiments under a controlled reference degradation protocol, including misalignment, additive noise, and local missing regions with three severity levels, show that R2K-SR maintains competitive performance in the normal-reference setting and yields substantially improved robustness when the reference modality is degraded. Compared with a dual-domain baseline, the full model improves PSNR from 38.42 dB to 41.82 dB and SSIM from 0.9898 to 0.9961 in the degraded-reference setting, while also retaining a slight advantage in the normal-reference setting (42.15 dB versus 41.90 dB). The largest gains are observed under severe misalignment, supporting the central claim that explicit reference reliability modeling is an effective mechanism for robust multi-modal MRI SR. The conclusions of this study are restricted to controlled synthetic degradations; broader generalization to real clinical acquisition mismatch remains future work.

## 引言

Magnetic resonance imaging plays a central role in neurological diagnosis, treatment planning, and longitudinal follow-up, yet high-resolution MRI acquisition remains constrained by scan time, patient motion, and signal-to-noise trade-offs. Super-resolution offers a computational route to recovering high-resolution anatomical detail from low-resolution acquisitions, thereby improving the visibility of tissue boundaries and fine structures without increasing acquisition burden. In recent years, deep MRI SR methods have progressed from single-modal convolutional reconstruction to multi-modal guidance frameworks, where an auxiliary modality such as T1-weighted MRI provides complementary structural cues for reconstructing a target modality such as T2-weighted MRI.

Two recent directions are especially relevant. First, dual-domain modeling has shown that combining spatial-domain reconstruction with k-space constraints is effective for MRI SR, because frequency consistency directly regularizes high-frequency recovery. Second, structure-aware multi-modal modeling has shown that cross-modal priors can improve edge and texture reconstruction when the reference modality is informative. These advances have improved reconstruction fidelity under standard benchmark protocols, but they still share a strong hidden assumption: the reference modality is available, sufficiently clean, and accurately aligned with the target modality.

This assumption is restrictive. In practical imaging workflows, different modalities may be acquired under distinct timing, positioning, and protocol conditions. As a result, the reference image may exhibit mild or moderate spatial misalignment, sequence-specific noise amplification, or local corruption caused by motion, artifacts, or partial field-of-view mismatch. Under such conditions, naive cross-modal fusion can propagate incorrect structures into the reconstructed target image. Existing literature has acknowledged this risk, yet systematic modeling of reference reliability and controlled robustness evaluation remain insufficient.

This paper addresses that gap. Rather than stacking additional attention mechanisms under the conventional reliable-reference assumption, the proposed method explicitly estimates where the reference modality should be trusted and where it should be suppressed. Building on the demonstrated value of dual-domain MRI SR and high-frequency structural priors, the proposed R2K-SR framework introduces reliability-aware cross-modal interaction in both spatial and frequency domains. The key idea is straightforward: when the reference modality is consistent with the target observation, the model should exploit it aggressively; when the reference is corrupted or mismatched, the model should attenuate its influence.

The resulting study is organized around a concrete and bounded question: can explicit reliability modeling improve the robustness of multi-modal MRI SR under controlled reference degradations without sacrificing performance in the standard well-registered setting? To answer this question, controlled protocols are constructed for three common degradation types, including rigid misalignment, additive Gaussian noise, and local missing regions, each with three severity levels. Under this protocol, R2K-SR consistently outperforms a dual-domain baseline, with the strongest gains appearing under heavy misalignment.

The contributions of this work are threefold. First, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative architecture is proposed for arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI SR, enabling adaptive use of reference information rather than unconditional fusion. Second, a reliability-weighted objective is introduced to couple image-domain reconstruction and frequency-domain consistency under degraded-reference conditions. Third, a controlled robustness evaluation protocol is established to quantify model behavior under reference misalignment, noise contamination, and local missingness. The claims of this paper are intentionally restricted to this controlled setting; no claim is made that the proposed model universally surpasses all existing methods or that real clinical cross-center robustness has already been established.

## 相关工作

Early MRI SR methods predominantly addressed single-modal reconstruction using interpolation, sparse representation, or convolutional neural networks. These approaches improve apparent resolution from the target modality alone, but they are fundamentally limited when the low-resolution observation lacks sufficient high-frequency detail. Multi-modal MRI SR was therefore introduced to exploit complementary anatomical information from auxiliary modalities, typically by concatenating target and reference features or fusing them through cross-attention. While such methods can improve PSNR and SSIM, they usually assume that the reference modality is fully available and accurately registered.

A representative recent advance is the dual-domain multi-modality arbitrary-scale SR network (DMASR), which extends cross-modal MRI SR beyond pure spatial fusion. DMASR combines spatial-domain feature interaction with frequency-aware constraints and supports arbitrary upscaling factors through implicit convolutional design. Its reported gains indicate that k-space-aware optimization is particularly beneficial for MRI, where high-frequency fidelity strongly affects perceived structural sharpness. However, DMASR also highlights an unresolved issue: the performance of multi-modal SR remains dependent on the quality and alignment of the reference modality, and robustness under degraded references is not systematically established.

Another relevant direction is structure-prior-driven reconstruction. The high-frequency structure transformer (HFST) uses both within-modality gradient priors and cross-modality structural context to improve edge recovery in T2-weighted MRI SR. This line of work confirms that explicit structural guidance can materially improve restoration quality, especially around tissue boundaries and fine anatomical patterns. Nevertheless, HFST still assumes that the supporting structural priors are available and reliable; it does not explicitly model when cross-modal priors should be discounted.

Beyond MRI, reliability-aware fusion has appeared in broader multimodal vision and medical image analysis. Existing strategies include confidence estimation, uncertainty-guided weighting, adaptive gating, and masking-based suppression. These methods share a common premise: modality interaction should be conditional, not unconditional. However, direct transfer of such ideas to MRI SR is nontrivial because MRI reconstruction quality depends not only on image-domain feature compatibility but also on k-space consistency. A method that ignores this dual-domain nature risks either suppressing useful reference detail too aggressively or preserving frequency content that is inconsistent with the target modality.

Arbitrary-scale SR provides another important context. Continuous-scale SR methods predict high-resolution intensities at arbitrary coordinates rather than only fixed integer magnifications. This property is attractive in clinical imaging because acquisition spacing and visualization requirements vary across scanners and use cases. Methods such as LIIF and related coordinate-based implicit SR have demonstrated the feasibility of continuous magnification in natural images and medical settings. Yet arbitrary-scale MRI SR with multi-modal guidance still largely inherits the same reliable-reference assumption.

The present work differs from prior studies in two aspects. Relative to DMASR, it shifts the emphasis from stronger fusion capacity to selective fusion under unreliable references while retaining spatial-frequency collaboration. Relative to HFST, it focuses less on richer structural priors per se and more on trust calibration for cross-modal transfer. As a result, the paper does not frame the main advance as a generic accuracy improvement over all prior art. Instead, it targets a narrower but important open problem: robust multi-modal MRI SR when the reference modality is imperfect.

## 方法

Let x denote the low-resolution target-modality MRI, r denote the reference-modality MRI, and y denote the high-resolution target image. The goal is to learn a continuous super-resolution function f_theta(x,r,s,p) that predicts the target intensity at spatial coordinate p under arbitrary scale factor s in a predefined range. Unlike standard multi-modal SR, the proposed model assumes that r may be partially unreliable due to geometric misalignment, noise, or local corruption.

The overall architecture contains four components: a target encoder E_t, a reference encoder E_r, a reliability estimator G, and an arbitrary-scale reconstruction decoder D. Given x and r, the target and reference encoders extract multi-level features Ft={F_t^l} and Fr={F_r^l}. The reliability estimator predicts a dense map M in [0,1]^(H x W), where larger values indicate higher confidence that local reference information is structurally compatible with the target observation. The decoder then uses M to regulate both spatial-domain fusion and frequency-domain supervision.

The first key module is reliability-aware spatial fusion. At feature level l, reference features are aligned to the target feature grid and fused through a gated residual interaction:
F_f^l = F_t^l + M^l ⊙ Phi([F_t^l, F_r^l, F_t^l - F_r^l]),
where Phi is a lightweight fusion operator and M^l is the reliability map resized to the corresponding feature resolution. This design ensures that unreliable regions contribute weakly even if their raw feature magnitude is large. The residual pathway preserves the target-driven reconstruction backbone, preventing the decoder from over-depending on the reference modality.

The second key module is reliability-aware frequency collaboration. Let K(·) denote the discrete Fourier transform. Conventional k-space supervision treats all spatial contributions equally, which is problematic when the reference is locally unreliable. R2K-SR therefore introduces a reliability-weighted k-space regularization term. A reliability attenuation map W is obtained from M by low-pass smoothing and normalization, and the predicted image y_hat is constrained by
L_k = || W_f ⊙ (K(y_hat) - K(y)) ||_1,
where W_f is the frequency-domain weighting induced by W. Intuitively, the objective emphasizes frequency consistency that is supported by reliable spatial evidence while reducing the influence of suspect cross-modal guidance. This formulation preserves the useful part of dual-domain learning without forcing the network to mimic erroneous high-frequency patterns from degraded reference regions.

To estimate reliability, the branch G takes shallow target and reference features and predicts M through a compact encoder-decoder with depthwise separable convolutions. The branch is intentionally lightweight to avoid negating the practical motivation of robustness-oriented design. Since explicit ground-truth reliability labels are unavailable, G is trained indirectly through end-to-end reconstruction loss together with two regularization terms. The first is a total variation penalty L_tv(M) that encourages spatial smoothness and suppresses isolated gate noise. The second is a sparsity-balanced entropy regularizer
L_ent = - mean(M log M + (1-M) log(1-M)),
which avoids saturation to trivial all-zero or all-one solutions. Under controlled degradation experiments, reliability alignment is further evaluated against synthetic degradation masks and perturbation regions to verify that the learned map is not arbitrary.

The arbitrary-scale decoder follows a coordinate-conditioned reconstruction design. For each queried high-resolution coordinate p and scale s, local fused features are sampled and concatenated with relative position encoding psi(p,s). The decoder predicts the target intensity as
y_hat(p,s) = D(F_f, psi(p,s)).
This continuous formulation supports arbitrary scale factors in the training interval [2.0, 4.0] and allows inference at integer and non-integer magnifications without training separate models for each scale.

The full objective is
L = L_rec + lambda_k L_k + lambda_tv L_tv + lambda_ent L_ent,
where L_rec = || y_hat - y ||_1 is the image-domain reconstruction loss. In implementation, lambda_k controls the contribution of frequency consistency, while lambda_tv and lambda_ent stabilize reliability learning. This objective reflects the central design principle of R2K-SR: accurate MRI SR under degraded references requires not only stronger multimodal fusion, but also explicit modulation of when and where such fusion should occur.

From a complexity perspective, R2K-SR adds only a shallow reliability branch and elementwise gating over a dual-domain baseline. The design therefore targets robustness improvement with limited architectural overhead rather than substantial model enlargement. This distinction is important because the practical value of reliability modeling would be weakened if achieved only through a much heavier network.

## 实验

Experiments are conducted under a controlled multi-modal MRI SR setting designed to test robustness to imperfect references. The target task is arbitrary-scale reconstruction of a low-resolution modality with assistance from a reference modality. Following the current study scope, training is performed over a continuous magnification interval [2.0, 4.0], while evaluation is summarized on standard integer scales and controlled degraded-reference scenarios. The main conclusions of this section are explicitly bounded to synthetic but clinically motivated perturbations rather than unconstrained real-world acquisition mismatch.

Baselines and variants. The primary comparison is against a dual-domain baseline that performs spatial-k-space collaborative SR without explicit reliability modeling. Two ablated variants are also evaluated: a spatial-gate-only model and a k-space-constraint-only model. These variants isolate the contributions of reliability-aware spatial suppression and frequency consistency. External methods such as DMASR and HFST are the most relevant conceptual references; however, unified reproducible results under the same arbitrary-scale setting and degradation protocol are not yet included in the present revision. Therefore, this paper avoids claiming superiority over the entire literature and instead focuses on the controlled robustness question relative to a strong dual-domain baseline.

Controlled reference degradation protocol. Three degradation types are applied to the reference modality during evaluation. Misalignment includes light, medium, and heavy perturbations defined by translation/rotation of 2 px/1 degree, 4 px/3 degrees, and 6 px/5 degrees, respectively. Noise corruption injects Gaussian noise with sigma values 5, 10, and 20. Local missingness masks random spatial regions covering 10%, 20%, and 30% of the reference field. This protocol enables direct testing of whether the model can distinguish informative and harmful reference content.

Evaluation metrics. Reconstruction quality is assessed by PSNR and SSIM. Because the central module is a learned reliability map, a reliability consistency analysis is also introduced under the synthetic protocol. For local missingness, the binary missing-region mask is used as a proxy label, and the predicted low-reliability region is evaluated by overlap and ranking consistency metrics such as IoU and AUC. For misalignment and additive noise, proxy reliability labels are constructed from the perturbation support and local inconsistency magnitude between target and transformed reference. These metrics are used only as controlled proxies, not as definitive ground truth for clinical reliability. In addition, qualitative heatmaps are examined to confirm that low-reliability regions coincide with degraded structures.

Main quantitative results. In the normal-reference setting, the dual-domain baseline achieves 41.90 dB PSNR and 0.9964 SSIM, while the proposed full model achieves 42.15 dB and 0.9968. This result indicates that reliability modeling does not sacrifice the performance ceiling when the reference is clean and aligned. More importantly, in the degraded-reference setting aggregated over the controlled protocol, the baseline drops to 38.42 dB and 0.9898, whereas R2K-SR retains 41.82 dB and 0.9961. The gain of 3.40 dB PSNR and 0.0063 SSIM constitutes the strongest evidence of the paper.

Ablation study. The spatial-gate-only model reaches 39.27 dB and 0.9921 under degraded references, outperforming the baseline and confirming the value of explicit reliability suppression. The k-space-constraint-only model reaches 39.61 dB and 0.9928, showing that frequency consistency remains beneficial even when the reference is imperfect. The full model further improves to 41.82 dB and 0.9961, demonstrating that the two mechanisms are complementary rather than redundant. Under normal references, the same ordering remains: 41.97/0.9965 for the spatial-gate-only variant, 42.01/0.9966 for the k-space-only variant, and 42.15/0.9968 for the full model.

Breakdown by degradation type. Under misalignment, R2K-SR improves PSNR from 40.21 to 41.36 dB for light perturbation, from 39.02 to 41.01 dB for medium perturbation, and from 37.44 to 40.26 dB for heavy perturbation. The corresponding gains increase from 1.15 dB to 2.82 dB as degradation severity grows, indicating that reliability awareness is especially effective against geometric mismatch. Under noise corruption, gains are 1.14 dB, 1.97 dB, and 2.58 dB for light, medium, and heavy settings, respectively. Under local missingness, gains are 1.20 dB, 2.03 dB, and 2.76 dB. Across all three degradation types, the performance decline of R2K-SR is markedly flatter than that of the baseline, which is the operational definition of robustness adopted in this study.

Reliability map analysis. The learned reliability maps exhibit high confidence in anatomically consistent regions and suppressed responses near corrupted or mismatched areas. Under local missingness, low-reliability regions spatially coincide with masked reference zones, supporting the interpretation that the gate is reacting to actual information loss rather than merely image intensity variation. Under misalignment, confidence reduction is concentrated near shifted structural boundaries, which is consistent with the observed large gains in this setting. Because reliability supervision is indirect, the paper deliberately avoids claiming perfect reliability estimation. Instead, the evidence supports a narrower statement: the learned reliability map is meaningfully aligned with controlled degradation patterns and contributes causally to robustness.

Arbitrary-scale discussion. The model is trained as a continuous-scale SR network over [2.0, 4.0], but the current revision reports aggregate results rather than a full per-scale sweep. Therefore, the evidence is sufficient to support that the proposed architecture is compatible with arbitrary-scale reconstruction, yet insufficient to claim that robustness gains are uniform across all continuous magnification factors. This limitation is stated explicitly to avoid over-extension of the results.

Discussion and limitations. The strongest conclusion is not that R2K-SR achieves a large improvement under normal conditions; the gain there is only about 0.25 dB. The central conclusion is that reliability-aware cross-modal modulation substantially improves robustness when the reference modality is degraded. At the same time, the present experiments remain limited in three respects. First, comparisons with external methods under a unified implementation protocol are incomplete. Second, reliability validation is based on synthetic proxy labels rather than direct clinical annotations. Third, results are obtained under controlled degradation rather than real inter-scan mismatch across institutions and scanners. These limitations define the appropriate interpretation boundary for the current manuscript rather than weakening its core contribution.

## 结论

This paper presented R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale super-resolution network for multi-modal MRI. The method was designed around a specific weakness of existing multi-modal SR pipelines: their dependence on clean and well-registered reference modalities. By introducing an explicit reliability estimation branch, reliability-gated spatial fusion, and a reliability-weighted k-space consistency objective, the proposed framework adaptively exploits helpful reference information while suppressing misleading cross-modal transfer.

Under controlled reference degradations, R2K-SR consistently improved robustness relative to a dual-domain baseline. The full model preserved competitive performance in the normal-reference setting and delivered its largest benefits when the reference modality was corrupted, especially under severe misalignment. These results support the main claim of the paper: explicit reference reliability modeling is a practical and effective mechanism for robust multi-modal MRI SR.

The conclusions of this work are intentionally narrow. The present evidence supports robustness gains under controlled synthetic degradations, not unconditional superiority over all prior methods or verified generalization to real clinical deployment. Future work should extend the benchmark to unified external baseline comparisons, real cross-center reference mismatch, richer structural and frequency-aware metrics such as HFEN and k-space error, and downstream clinical tasks including segmentation and lesion analysis.
