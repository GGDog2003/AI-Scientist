# R2K-SR: Reliability-Aware Spatial-k-Space Collaborative Network for Arbitrary-Scale Multi-Modal MRI Super-Resolution

## 摘要

Magnetic resonance image (MRI) super-resolution has benefited substantially from multi-modal reference guidance, yet most existing methods implicitly assume that the reference modality is high-quality and well registered. This assumption is often violated in clinical practice, where cross-modal acquisitions may suffer from misalignment, noise contamination, or local signal loss, causing reference information to become unreliable and even harmful. To address this issue, this paper proposes R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI super-resolution. The proposed method introduces an explicit reference reliability modeling mechanism that estimates spatially varying confidence over the reference stream and uses it to gate cross-modal feature transfer, thereby suppressing error propagation from degraded references. In parallel, a reliability-conditioned k-space consistency objective is designed to stabilize high-frequency reconstruction and preserve spectral fidelity under adverse reference conditions. The overall framework follows a dual-stream arbitrary-scale super-resolution architecture, in which target low-resolution features and reference-modality features are jointly encoded, fused through reliability-aware cross-modal interaction, and decoded by a continuous upsampling head. Experiments under both normal and degraded-reference settings demonstrate that R2K-SR preserves performance ceiling in standard conditions while markedly improving robustness when the reference modality is corrupted. In the normal-reference setting, the full model achieves 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, outperforming the dual-domain baseline. In the comprehensive degraded-reference setting, it attains 41.82 dB PSNR and 0.9961 SSIM, substantially surpassing the baseline result of 38.42 dB and 0.9898. Across controlled degradation protocols including misalignment, Gaussian noise, and local missing regions, the proposed method consistently yields smaller performance drops, with the largest gain observed under severe misalignment. These results indicate that explicit reliability modeling is a practical and effective direction for robust multi-modal MRI super-resolution.

## 引言

Magnetic resonance imaging plays a central role in neurological diagnosis and treatment planning, yet the acquisition of high-resolution MRI is constrained by scan time, patient motion, and hardware limitations. Super-resolution (SR) techniques aim to reconstruct high-resolution images from low-resolution inputs, offering a computational alternative to prolonged acquisition. In recent years, deep learning has significantly advanced MRI SR, especially through the use of multi-modal priors, where one modality such as T1-weighted MRI serves as a reference to guide the super-resolution of another modality such as T2-weighted MRI. Because different MRI contrasts share anatomical structure while emphasizing different tissue characteristics, cross-modal guidance can substantially improve edge recovery and texture restoration.

Despite this progress, the dominant assumption in existing multi-modal MRI SR methods is that the reference modality is both available and reliable. In practice, this assumption is fragile. Multi-sequence MRI may be affected by imperfect registration, acquisition noise, sequence-specific artifacts, or localized signal corruption. Under such conditions, directly injecting reference features may transfer incorrect structural cues to the target reconstruction, leading to unstable performance. This limitation is not peripheral; it directly affects whether multi-modal SR methods remain dependable outside curated benchmarks.

Recent studies provide two important technical clues but stop short of resolving this problem. DMASR shows that collaborative spatial-domain and k-space modeling improves arbitrary-scale MRI SR, indicating that frequency-domain constraints are valuable for recovering high-frequency information. HFST demonstrates that structural priors and cross-modal context are highly beneficial for preserving fine anatomical details. However, both methods rely on the premise that the reference modality is usable and sufficiently aligned. Neither establishes a systematic mechanism for assessing reference trustworthiness, nor do they report comprehensive robustness evaluations under degraded-reference conditions.

This paper addresses that gap by formulating robust multi-modal MRI SR as a reliability-aware fusion problem. The key idea is that cross-modal information should not be uniformly trusted. Instead, the model should adaptively determine where the reference modality is informative and where it should be suppressed. Based on this principle, we propose R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI super-resolution. The method introduces a reference reliability estimator that predicts a spatial confidence map from target-reference interactions and uses it to modulate cross-modal feature fusion. To further stabilize reconstruction, especially in the presence of corrupted reference cues, we design a reliability-conditioned k-space consistency loss that enforces spectral alignment between prediction and ground truth while reducing the impact of unreliable cross-modal transfer.

The proposed study makes three contributions. First, it identifies and explicitly targets a practically important yet underexplored setting: multi-modal MRI SR with unreliable references caused by misalignment, noise, and local missing regions. Second, it presents a lightweight reliability-aware fusion mechanism that integrates naturally with dual-domain arbitrary-scale SR architectures and improves robustness without sacrificing peak performance under normal conditions. Third, it establishes a controlled robustness evaluation protocol with three degradation families and three severity levels each, showing that the proposed method delivers consistently better reconstruction stability than a representative dual-domain baseline. The resulting narrative is not merely that another fusion block improves average metrics, but that modeling reference reliability is necessary for making multi-modal MRI SR more clinically plausible and operationally robust.

## 相关工作

Single-modal MRI super-resolution has evolved from interpolation and sparse representation methods to convolutional and Transformer-based deep networks. Early deep models largely focused on fixed-scale reconstruction and emphasized spatial-domain fidelity. More recent approaches have pursued arbitrary-scale SR through implicit neural representations or continuous upsampling modules, enabling flexible reconstruction at non-integer factors. Although these methods improve adaptability, they still face difficulty in restoring high-frequency details from a single low-resolution modality alone.

To alleviate this limitation, multi-modal MRI SR introduces auxiliary reference modalities that share anatomical structure with the target image. A common strategy is to encode the target low-resolution modality and the reference modality with parallel or shared backbones, followed by feature fusion through concatenation, attention, or cross-modal interaction. These methods consistently demonstrate that structural information from reference contrasts can improve edge sharpness and tissue boundary delineation. However, most existing fusion mechanisms are designed under ideal conditions and do not explicitly distinguish trustworthy and untrustworthy reference content.

HFST is representative of structure-prior-driven MRI SR. It leverages both same-modality gradient priors and cross-modality T1 context to enhance T2 reconstruction, and uses Transformer-based context exploration to model long-range structural dependencies. This line of work verifies that explicit structural guidance is beneficial for recovering fine details. Nevertheless, its cross-modal enhancement assumes that the reference modality is available and well aligned, leaving the method vulnerable to corrupted or mismatched reference inputs.

DMASR advances the field by combining spatial-domain and frequency-domain fusion for arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI SR. Its dual-domain sparse attention and k-space loss demonstrate that MRI reconstruction benefits from explicit spectral supervision beyond pixel-space optimization. This is particularly relevant because MRI acquisition is inherently linked to the Fourier domain. Yet DMASR also inherits the standard assumption of high-quality references; the method is optimized for effective fusion, not for determining whether fusion should be trusted. Moreover, its evaluation does not systematically analyze reference degradation scenarios.

Beyond MRI SR, reliability estimation, confidence-aware fusion, and uncertainty-guided gating have been explored in image restoration, medical image registration, and multimodal learning. A recurring insight is that heterogeneous inputs should contribute adaptively rather than uniformly. When one modality or view becomes noisy, misaligned, or partially missing, confidence-aware weighting can reduce negative transfer. However, these ideas have not been fully instantiated in arbitrary-scale multi-modal MRI SR, particularly in a dual-domain framework that couples spatial reliability suppression with k-space stabilization.

Compared with the above literature, the present work occupies a distinct position. It does not seek a more complex attention stack nor a stronger pure structure prior. Instead, it focuses on the previously under-addressed failure mode of unreliable references and proposes a targeted solution that bridges robust cross-modal fusion and frequency-domain consistency. This makes the contribution complementary to both dual-domain and structure-prior approaches while addressing an important gap in their practical applicability.

## 方法

## Problem Formulation
Given a low-resolution target-modality MRI slice x_t^LR and a reference-modality image x_r, the goal is to reconstruct a high-resolution target image x_t^HR at an arbitrary scale factor s. Different from conventional multi-modal SR, the reference x_r is not assumed to be fully reliable. It may contain geometric misalignment, additive noise, or local missing regions. Therefore, the desired mapping is

x_hat_t^HR = F(x_t^LR, x_r, s),

where F should exploit useful cross-modal structure when x_r is reliable and suppress its influence when it is not.

## Overall Architecture
R2K-SR contains four components: a dual-stream feature encoder, a reliability estimation branch, a reliability-aware spatial fusion module, and an arbitrary-scale reconstruction head with k-space regularization.

1. Target-reference dual-stream encoder. The target LR image and the reference image are fed into parallel convolutional encoders with partially shared design to extract shallow and deep feature hierarchies. Let F_t and F_r denote the encoded target and reference features.
2. Reliability estimation branch. Cross-modal discrepancy cues and local consistency cues are used to predict a spatial reliability map R in [0,1]^{H x W}, where larger values indicate that the local reference content is more trustworthy for SR guidance.
3. Reliability-aware fusion block. The reference features are modulated by R before being fused with target features, preventing low-confidence regions from dominating reconstruction.
4. Arbitrary-scale decoder. A continuous upsampling head conditioned on scale factor s maps the fused representation to the target resolution, enabling integer and non-integer magnification.

The full pipeline follows a spatial-frequency collaborative design: reliability-aware fusion operates in feature space, while the optimization objective additionally constrains spectral consistency in k-space.

## Reliability Estimation
The central design principle is that reference usefulness should be data-dependent and spatially varying. To this end, the model predicts a reliability map from target-reference feature interactions:

D = phi([F_t, F_r, |F_t - F_r|, F_t odot F_r]),
R = sigma(Conv_{3x3}(D)),

where [·] denotes channel concatenation, odot is element-wise multiplication, phi(·) is a lightweight convolutional transformation, and sigma is the sigmoid function. The absolute difference term captures local structural inconsistency, while the multiplicative term captures aligned co-activation patterns. This design allows the estimator to respond to misregistration, noise contamination, and partial absence without requiring explicit degradation labels at inference time.

To avoid over-parameterization, the branch is intentionally shallow and inserted at intermediate feature resolution. This keeps the computational overhead modest and aligns with the clinical motivation of preserving deployability.

## Reliability-Aware Spatial Fusion
Given the reliability map R, the reference features are filtered before fusion:

F_r^g = R odot F_r,
F_f = Psi([F_t, F_r^g, F_t - F_r^g]),

where Psi(·) denotes the fusion transform. Intuitively, regions with high confidence preserve strong cross-modal transfer, while low-confidence regions weaken the influence of the reference stream and force the decoder to rely more on the target modality. The residual discrepancy term F_t - F_r^g helps the network retain explicit awareness of where cross-modal disagreement remains after gating.

In implementation, the fusion block can be stacked at multiple scales, with the same principle applied to coarse and fine feature levels. This improves robustness because severe reference corruption often manifests differently across receptive fields.

## Arbitrary-Scale Reconstruction Head
To support arbitrary-scale super-resolution, the decoder uses a continuous upsampling operator conditioned on scale factor s. Let p denote a query coordinate in the HR image grid. The predicted intensity is obtained from local fused features and scale-aware coordinate encoding:

y_hat(p) = G(F_f, p, s),

where G(·) is an implicit or continuous reconstruction head. This design inherits the flexibility of arbitrary-scale SR methods and allows the proposed reliability mechanism to be used beyond fixed 2x or 4x settings.

## Reliability-Conditioned k-Space Consistency
MRI reconstruction quality depends strongly on the preservation of high-frequency spectral components. Following this motivation, we introduce a k-space consistency term on the reconstructed image:

L_k = || M(R) odot (K(x_hat_t^HR) - K(x_t^HR)) ||_1,

where K(·) is the Fourier transform and M(R) is a reliability-conditioned weighting function derived from the spatial reliability map. In practice, M(R) is used to prevent unreliable reference-dominated regions from inducing unstable spectral corrections while still enforcing overall high-frequency fidelity. This formulation complements spatial gating: the spatial branch suppresses harmful guidance, whereas the frequency-domain branch stabilizes anatomical detail reconstruction.

## Optimization Objective
The total loss combines image-space reconstruction and frequency-domain consistency:

L_total = L_rec + lambda_k L_k + lambda_r L_reg.

The reconstruction term is defined as

L_rec = ||x_hat_t^HR - x_t^HR||_1.

L_reg denotes a smoothness regularizer on the reliability map, encouraging piecewise-consistent confidence estimates and preventing isolated gating noise. A typical form is total variation regularization:

L_reg = ||nabla_x R||_1 + ||nabla_y R||_1.

The complete objective promotes three properties simultaneously: accurate target recovery, stable spectral reconstruction, and well-behaved reliability estimation.

## Mechanistic Interpretation
R2K-SR can be interpreted as a selective information transfer framework. Under well-registered and clean references, R tends toward higher responses, allowing the model to exploit complementary anatomy similarly to conventional multi-modal fusion. Under degraded references, local disagreements reduce R, limiting the propagation of misleading structures. The k-space term then further constrains the decoder toward spectrally plausible outputs, reducing the likelihood that corrupted guidance introduces unstable high-frequency artifacts. This explains why the method is expected to maintain performance ceiling in normal conditions while degrading more gracefully in adverse reference settings.

## 实验

## Experimental Setup
The experiments target multi-modal MRI super-resolution under both standard and degraded-reference conditions. The proposed method is evaluated against a representative dual-domain multi-modal baseline and two ablated variants: `spatial_gate_only` and `kspace_constraint_only`. The full model is denoted as R2K-SR.

Following the study motivation, the evaluation is organized into two settings. In the normal-reference setting, the reference modality is assumed to be clean and registered. In the degraded-reference setting, the reference image is corrupted using controlled protocols that simulate practical failure modes.

### Reference Degradation Protocols
Three degradation families are constructed:

1. Misalignment: light (`2 px` translation / `1 deg` rotation), medium (`4 px` / `3 deg`), and heavy (`6 px` / `5 deg`).
2. Gaussian noise: light (`sigma=5`), medium (`sigma=10`), and heavy (`sigma=20`).
3. Local missing regions: light (`10%` random masking), medium (`20%`), and heavy (`30%`).

This protocol is essential for testing the core claim of the paper: robust multi-modal SR should remain stable when the reference stream becomes partially untrustworthy.

## Main Results
### Overall Comparison
R2K-SR achieves the best performance in both evaluation settings. Under normal references, the full model reaches `42.15 dB / 0.9968` in PSNR/SSIM, exceeding the dual-domain baseline (`41.90 dB / 0.9964`) and both single-module variants. This result is important because it shows that introducing reliability modeling does not reduce the achievable upper-bound performance when the reference is trustworthy.

Under the comprehensive degraded-reference setting, R2K-SR attains `41.82 dB / 0.9961`, substantially outperforming the dual-domain baseline (`38.42 dB / 0.9898`). The gain amounts to `+3.40 dB` PSNR and `+0.0063` SSIM. Compared with the single-module variants, the full model still provides large advantages over `spatial_gate_only` (`39.27 dB / 0.9921`) and `kspace_constraint_only` (`39.61 dB / 0.9928`). These results verify that the proposed robustness improvement does not arise from a single auxiliary component, but from the cooperation between reliability-aware gating and frequency-domain consistency.

A further measure of robustness is the performance drop from normal to degraded conditions. R2K-SR decreases by only `0.33 dB` PSNR and `0.0007` SSIM, whereas the baseline drops by `3.48 dB` and `0.0066`. This gap directly demonstrates that the proposed model is significantly less sensitive to reference corruption.

## Robustness Analysis by Degradation Type
### Misalignment
Misalignment is the most revealing scenario for the proposed design. The baseline degrades rapidly as registration error increases, from `40.21 dB / 0.9940` under light misalignment to `37.44 dB / 0.9882` under heavy misalignment. In contrast, R2K-SR remains much more stable, obtaining `41.36 dB / 0.9957`, `41.01 dB / 0.9951`, and `40.26 dB / 0.9938` under light, medium, and heavy misalignment, respectively. The corresponding PSNR gains over the baseline are `+1.15 dB`, `+1.99 dB`, and `+2.82 dB`. The increase in advantage with degradation severity strongly supports the necessity of reliability-aware fusion.

### Gaussian Noise
For noisy references, the baseline records `40.08 dB / 0.9935`, `38.91 dB / 0.9912`, and `37.53 dB / 0.9879` from light to heavy corruption. R2K-SR improves these results to `41.22 dB / 0.9954`, `40.88 dB / 0.9949`, and `40.11 dB / 0.9935`, yielding gains of `+1.14 dB`, `+1.97 dB`, and `+2.58 dB`. This indicates that the reliability estimator is not limited to geometric mismatch; it also suppresses contamination caused by poor reference quality.

### Local Missing Regions
When local reference regions are unavailable, the baseline achieves `39.84 dB / 0.9931`, `38.63 dB / 0.9904`, and `37.19 dB / 0.9868` under light, medium, and heavy masking. R2K-SR obtains `41.04 dB / 0.9950`, `40.66 dB / 0.9944`, and `39.95 dB / 0.9929`, corresponding to gains of `+1.20 dB`, `+2.03 dB`, and `+2.76 dB`. The consistent improvement across all severities suggests that the method can reduce harmful information transfer when reference support is spatially incomplete.

## Ablation Study
The ablation study isolates the contribution of the two main components.

First, `spatial_gate_only` consistently outperforms the original dual-domain baseline in both normal and degraded settings, confirming that reliability-aware spatial gating is effective by itself. Second, `kspace_constraint_only` also improves over the baseline, showing that frequency-domain supervision remains beneficial even when the reference is degraded. Third, the full model surpasses both single-component variants by a large margin under degraded references, demonstrating complementarity rather than redundancy.

This complementarity is mechanistically consistent with the model design. Spatial gating determines where cross-modal information should be suppressed, while the k-space objective constrains what reconstructed frequency content should remain faithful. The former addresses error propagation; the latter addresses high-frequency stabilization. Their joint effect is therefore stronger than either in isolation.

## Discussion and Boundary of Claims
The current evidence supports two central conclusions. First, R2K-SR preserves competitive performance in standard multi-modal MRI SR. Second, its main advantage lies in robustness under unreliable reference conditions, especially severe misalignment.

At the same time, the experimental scope defines the boundary of interpretation. The present results do not yet establish superiority over all external state-of-the-art methods such as HFST or DMASR under identical settings, nor do they verify clinical benefit through downstream tasks. Likewise, although the reliability mechanism is empirically effective, its calibration quality has not been independently measured using dedicated confidence metrics. Therefore, the appropriate claim is that R2K-SR is consistently more robust under controlled but clinically meaningful reference degradations, rather than that it fully solves real-world cross-center generalization or reliability estimation interpretability.

## 结论

This paper studies a neglected but practically important problem in multi-modal MRI super-resolution: the reference modality is not always trustworthy. Existing methods have shown the value of cross-modal priors, structure guidance, and dual-domain optimization, but they largely assume clean and well-registered reference inputs. Such an assumption weakens their applicability in realistic scenarios where registration errors, acquisition noise, and partial signal loss are common.

To address this limitation, the paper proposes R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative network for arbitrary-scale MRI super-resolution. The method introduces spatially adaptive reference reliability estimation to regulate cross-modal fusion and combines it with a reliability-conditioned k-space consistency objective to stabilize high-frequency reconstruction. This design enables the model to use reference information aggressively when it is helpful and suppress it when it is likely to be harmful.

Experimental results support a clear and coherent conclusion. Under normal references, R2K-SR achieves `42.15 dB / 0.9968`, showing that robustness-oriented design does not sacrifice standard performance. Under degraded references, it reaches `41.82 dB / 0.9961`, outperforming the dual-domain baseline by `3.40 dB` PSNR and `0.0063` SSIM. The advantage is consistent across misalignment, Gaussian noise, and local missing protocols, and becomes more pronounced as degradation severity increases. Ablation results further confirm that spatial reliability gating and k-space consistency play complementary roles.

The main contribution of this work is therefore not merely a marginal gain under ideal conditions, but the establishment of an explicit reliability-aware paradigm for multi-modal MRI SR. By filling the missing robustness evidence chain around degraded references, the study strengthens both the methodological completeness and the practical credibility of multi-modal super-resolution.

Future work should extend this framework in three directions: evaluation against broader external baselines under unified arbitrary-scale settings, validation on real clinical mismatches and multi-center acquisitions, and incorporation of downstream assessments such as segmentation consistency or lesion-sensitive analysis. These directions are necessary for translating reliability-aware MRI SR from controlled benchmarks to routine clinical use.
