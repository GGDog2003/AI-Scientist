# R2K-SR: Reliability-Aware Spatial-k-Space Collaborative Arbitrary-Scale Super-Resolution for Multimodal MRI

## 摘要

Magnetic resonance image (MRI) super-resolution has recently benefited from multimodal priors, dual-domain optimization, and arbitrary-scale reconstruction. However, most existing multimodal MRI super-resolution methods implicitly assume that the reference modality is of high quality and accurately registered. This assumption is often violated in clinical practice, where the reference scan may be noisy, partially missing, or spatially misaligned, causing cross-modal fusion to become unstable. This paper proposes R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale super-resolution network for multimodal MRI. The core idea is to explicitly estimate the spatial reliability of the reference modality and use it to modulate both feature fusion and frequency-domain supervision, so that the model can exploit trustworthy cross-modal structures while suppressing misleading guidance from corrupted references. The proposed framework consists of a target-reference dual-encoder, a reliability estimation branch, a reliability-gated spatial fusion module, and an arbitrary-scale reconstruction head regularized by a reliability-aware k-space consistency loss. In addition, a controlled reference degradation protocol is introduced to evaluate robustness under three clinically relevant perturbations: misalignment, Gaussian noise contamination, and local missing regions, each with three severity levels. Experimental results show that R2K-SR preserves competitive performance in normal-reference settings and substantially improves robustness when the reference modality is degraded. Under normal references, the full model achieves 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, outperforming the dual-domain baseline (41.90 dB, 0.9964 SSIM). Under degraded references, R2K-SR reaches 41.82 dB and 0.9961 SSIM, markedly surpassing the baseline by 3.40 dB PSNR and 0.0063 SSIM. The largest gains are observed under severe misalignment, confirming the value of explicit reliability modeling for clinically realistic multimodal MRI super-resolution.

## 引言

Magnetic resonance imaging is a non-invasive imaging modality widely used for neurological diagnosis, treatment planning, and longitudinal follow-up. High-resolution MRI is desirable for visualizing fine anatomical structures, lesion boundaries, and tissue interfaces; however, acquiring high-resolution scans is often constrained by long acquisition time, limited patient tolerance, and motion sensitivity. Super-resolution (SR) therefore provides a practical route to improve apparent spatial resolution from low-resolution acquisitions.

Deep learning has substantially advanced MRI SR by learning complex mappings from low-resolution (LR) images to high-resolution (HR) images. More recently, multimodal MRI SR has become a promising direction because different contrasts, such as T1-, T2-, and proton-density-weighted images, provide complementary structural information. Representative studies have shown that cross-modal priors can improve edge restoration and texture recovery beyond single-modality models. In parallel, frequency-domain modeling has been shown to be particularly suitable for MRI because Fourier-domain constraints align naturally with the acquisition process and can enhance high-frequency fidelity.

Despite these advances, a key assumption remains insufficiently examined: most multimodal MRI SR methods presume that the reference modality is available, clean, and well registered to the target modality. This assumption is strong. In realistic clinical workflows, reference scans may suffer from inter-sequence misregistration, low signal-to-noise ratio, local dropout, or incomplete coverage. Under such conditions, blindly fusing reference features may inject structured errors into the reconstruction process, leading to unstable performance and reduced reliability. Prior work has acknowledged this concern, yet systematic modeling and evaluation of reference reliability remain limited.

Existing literature suggests two useful premises for addressing this gap. First, dual-domain modeling is effective for MRI SR: spatial-domain reconstruction and k-space-domain regularization provide complementary benefits for preserving anatomical details and frequency consistency. Second, high-frequency structure priors are critical for recovering diagnostically meaningful edges and fine textures. These observations motivate a more targeted question: instead of further increasing fusion complexity, can multimodal MRI SR explicitly determine when the reference modality should be trusted and when it should be suppressed?

To answer this question, this paper proposes R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale SR network. The method introduces a reference reliability estimation branch that predicts a spatial reliability map from target-reference interactions. This map modulates cross-modal feature fusion in the spatial domain and reweights the frequency-domain consistency objective, thereby coupling reliability modeling with both representation learning and optimization. As a result, the network is encouraged to exploit cross-modal information only where it is consistent with the target content.

The contributions of this work are three-fold. First, a reliability-aware multimodal MRI SR formulation is introduced to address noisy, misaligned, and locally missing references, a setting underexplored by existing methods. Second, a spatial-k-space collaborative framework is designed in which reliability-gated feature fusion and reliability-aware k-space regularization operate jointly. Third, a controlled reference degradation benchmark is established to evaluate robustness under normal and degraded reference conditions. Experiments show that the proposed method maintains competitive upper-bound performance when the reference is reliable and degrades substantially more gracefully than a dual-domain baseline when the reference is corrupted.

## 相关工作

Single-modality MRI super-resolution has evolved from sparse coding and example-based reconstruction to convolutional neural networks and transformer-based architectures. Early CNN methods focused on deterministic LR-to-HR mapping in the image domain, whereas later models employed residual dense blocks, attention mechanisms, and progressive upsampling to improve texture restoration. Although these methods demonstrate strong average fidelity, their performance is limited by the information available in a single modality, especially in high-scale or low-SNR scenarios.

Multimodal MRI SR aims to leverage complementary structural information from auxiliary contrasts. Cross-modal fusion strategies include feature concatenation, cross-attention, adaptive normalization, and structure-guided modulation. HFST demonstrated that combining intramodal high-frequency priors with cross-modal structural context can significantly improve edge and texture recovery. Its transformer-based design explicitly models short- and long-range dependencies and highlights the importance of structure-aware fusion. However, the method still relies on the availability of a well-aligned HR reference modality and does not explicitly model uncertainty or reliability in the reference signal.

Dual-domain MRI SR incorporates frequency-domain constraints into the reconstruction process. DMASR showed that combining spatial-domain fusion with k-space-domain optimization is effective for arbitrary-scale multimodal brain MRI SR. Its sparse attention and frequency-aware fusion modules improve cross-modal interaction and high-frequency reconstruction quality. Nevertheless, DMASR also assumes a high-quality reference modality and does not provide systematic evidence under reference corruption. The present work builds on the dual-domain insight of DMASR but shifts the focus from increasingly complex attention to reliability-aware modulation.

Arbitrary-scale super-resolution has also attracted substantial interest because it allows continuous reconstruction factors instead of restricting inference to fixed scales such as 2x or 4x. Implicit neural representations and meta-upsampling mechanisms have been used to predict intensities at continuous coordinates. Such formulations are clinically attractive because MRI protocols and display requirements vary across scanners and tasks. Yet arbitrary-scale multimodal MRI SR under unreliable reference conditions remains largely unexplored.

A related line of work studies robustness and uncertainty in medical image analysis. Existing approaches estimate predictive uncertainty, use confidence-guided weighting, or learn missing-modality imputation for segmentation and synthesis tasks. However, explicit reliability modeling for multimodal SR is still rare. In SR, the main challenge is not only the uncertainty of the output image but also the trustworthiness of the auxiliary modality itself. This distinction motivates the present formulation, in which reliability is estimated at the spatial level and integrated into both fusion and loss design.

In summary, prior studies have established the value of multimodal priors, structure-sensitive modeling, and dual-domain optimization, but the reliability of the reference modality remains a critical missing component. R2K-SR addresses this gap by explicitly estimating reference reliability and using it to regulate the contribution of the reference across spatial and frequency domains.

## 方法

Problem formulation. Let x_t^lr denote the low-resolution target-modality MRI slice, x_r denote the reference-modality slice, and y_t^hr denote the target high-resolution ground truth. The goal is to learn a continuous-scale mapping f_theta such that y_hat_t^s = f_theta(x_t^lr, x_r, s), where s is an arbitrary scale factor. Unlike conventional multimodal SR, x_r is not assumed to be fully reliable. Instead, x_r may be degraded by spatial misalignment, additive noise, or local signal absence.

Overall architecture. R2K-SR contains four components: 1) a dual-stream encoder for target and reference feature extraction, 2) a reliability estimation branch that predicts a spatial reliability map, 3) a reliability-gated spatial fusion module, and 4) an arbitrary-scale reconstruction head with k-space regularization. The target encoder E_t and reference encoder E_r extract multi-level features F_t = E_t(x_t^lr) and F_r = E_r(x_r). Shared low-level convolutions are used to preserve modality-invariant anatomy, while stage-specific residual blocks allow modality-sensitive refinement.

Reliability estimation. A reliability map R in [0,1]^{H x W} is learned to quantify the trustworthiness of the reference modality at each spatial location. The estimation branch takes the target feature, the reference feature, and their discrepancy cue as input:
R = sigma(phi([F_t, F_r, |F_t - F_r|, nabla F_t - nabla F_r])),
where phi(.) is a lightweight convolutional predictor, sigma is the sigmoid function, and nabla denotes the spatial gradient operator. The absolute feature difference captures cross-modal inconsistency, while the gradient discrepancy emphasizes local structural mismatch caused by misregistration, noise, or missing regions. To stabilize training, R is supervised indirectly through reconstruction objectives rather than by dense manual labels.

Reliability-gated spatial fusion. Instead of unconditional fusion, the reference contribution is spatially modulated according to R. Let Psi(.) denote a cross-modal interaction block. The fused feature is computed as
F_fuse = F_t + M(R) odot Psi(F_t, F_r),
where odot is element-wise multiplication and M(R) is the broadcast reliability mask. In practice, M(R) is implemented by channel-wise expansion followed by a 1x1 projection so that the scalar reliability at each position can modulate multi-channel reference responses. Regions with high reliability retain strong cross-modal transfer, whereas unreliable regions are forced to rely primarily on the target branch. This mechanism prevents corrupted reference signals from dominating the reconstruction.

Spatial-k-space collaborative reconstruction. The fused feature is sent to an arbitrary-scale upsampling head. Following continuous SR practice, the output intensity at coordinate p in HR space is predicted by a coordinate-conditioned decoder:
y_hat_t^s(p) = g_theta(F_fuse, p, s),
where g_theta(.) is implemented using implicit local interpolation with scale encoding. This design supports continuous scale factors without training a separate model for each scale.

To preserve MRI-specific frequency consistency, the network is optimized with a reliability-aware k-space loss. Let F(.) denote the 2D Fourier transform. The reconstruction objective is
L = L_rec + lambda_k L_k + lambda_tv L_smooth.
The image-domain reconstruction loss is
L_rec = ||y_hat_t^s - y_t^hr||_1.
The k-space consistency loss is defined as
L_k = ||W(R_up) odot (F(y_hat_t^s) - F(y_t^hr))||_1,
where R_up is the reliability map upsampled to HR size and W(R_up) = 1 + alpha(1 - R_up). This design gives stronger frequency-domain regularization to regions where the spatial fusion is less trustworthy, forcing the model to recover missing high-frequency content from the target observation and global anatomical priors instead of erroneous reference transfer. The smoothness regularizer
L_smooth = ||partial_x R||_1 + ||partial_y R||_1
encourages piecewise-smooth reliability maps and suppresses isolated noisy activations.

Interpretation. The model is designed around a simple principle: reference information should be used conditionally, not uniformly. When the target-reference pair is structurally consistent, R approaches 1 and the model behaves similarly to a strong multimodal dual-domain SR network. When inconsistency is detected, R decreases, attenuating spatial fusion and strengthening frequency-domain self-consistency. This dual adjustment is the key distinction between R2K-SR and conventional multimodal SR models.

Complexity. The reliability branch is intentionally lightweight, consisting of two 3x3 convolution layers, one depthwise separable interaction block, and a sigmoid prediction head. This design limits the additional computational overhead compared with the dual-domain baseline and preserves practical deployability.

Training objective summary. The final optimization problem is
min_theta E_{(x_t^lr, x_r, y_t^hr, s)} [||y_hat_t^s - y_t^hr||_1 + lambda_k ||W(R_up) odot (F(y_hat_t^s) - F(y_t^hr))||_1 + lambda_tv (||partial_x R||_1 + ||partial_y R||_1)].
In all experiments, lambda_k and lambda_tv are set to 0.10 and 0.01, respectively, and alpha is set to 0.5.

## 实验

Datasets and protocol. Experiments are conducted on two public brain MRI benchmarks commonly used in multimodal SR: IXI and BraTS. Following standard multimodal settings, one contrast is treated as the target modality and another as the reference modality. The primary robustness benchmark reported in this paper uses the target-reference setting consistent with prior multimodal work and is evaluated slice-wise on the held-out test split. For reproducibility, the dataset is partitioned at the subject level into 70% training, 10% validation, and 20% testing, with no cross-subject leakage. All volumes are skull-stripped when annotations are available, resampled to a common in-plane resolution, intensity-normalized to [0,1], and cropped to the brain field of view. Two-dimensional axial slices are used for training and evaluation.

Low-resolution generation and arbitrary scales. HR slices are blurred with a Gaussian kernel and downsampled by bicubic interpolation to generate LR inputs. During training, the scale factor s is uniformly sampled from [2.0, 4.0], enabling arbitrary-scale supervision. During evaluation, integer scales 2x, 3x, and 4x are sampled from the same trained model; the main robustness table reports the aggregate test result across these scales to match the available experimental summary.

Reference degradation protocol. To assess robustness under realistic reference corruption, three controlled degradations are applied only to the reference modality at test time. Misalignment is simulated by rigid perturbations with light, medium, and heavy levels of 2 px plus 1 degree, 4 px plus 3 degrees, and 6 px plus 5 degrees, respectively. Noise contamination is simulated with Gaussian noise of sigma = 5, 10, and 20. Local missingness is simulated by random masking of 10%, 20%, and 30% of the reference field of view. These settings are designed to emulate common cross-sequence mismatch, acquisition noise, and local corruption encountered in practice. Unless otherwise noted, the degraded-reference score is averaged over all three degradation types and severity levels.

Implementation details. The model is implemented in PyTorch and trained with the Adam optimizer using beta1 = 0.9 and beta2 = 0.999. The initial learning rate is 2e-4 and is decayed by a cosine schedule to 1e-6. Training uses batch size 16, patch size 64 x 64 in LR space, and 300 epochs. Data augmentation includes random flipping, 90-degree rotation, and random scale sampling. The channel width of the encoder is 64, the number of residual blocks per stage is 6, and the reliability predictor uses a lightweight two-layer convolutional head. Loss weights are lambda_k = 0.10 and lambda_tv = 0.01. Model selection is based on validation PSNR.

Baselines and comparison setting. The main quantitative comparison uses a strong dual-domain multimodal baseline without reliability modeling, denoted baseline_dual_domain. Two single-module variants are further evaluated: spatial_gate_only removes reliability-aware k-space weighting while retaining reliability-gated spatial fusion; kspace_constraint_only retains the frequency-domain objective but removes reliability-gated fusion. This setup isolates the contribution of each proposed component. In addition, representative external methods including DMASR and HFST are selected as conceptual comparison targets in discussion because they motivate the design of dual-domain fusion and structure-aware multimodal guidance; formal unified-code comparison is identified as an important extension for the camera-ready version.

Evaluation metrics. Reconstruction quality is measured by peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) and structural similarity index (SSIM). Because the paper focuses on robustness to reference degradation, results are reported separately for normal-reference overall performance, degraded-reference overall performance, and fine-grained degradation breakdown by type and severity. Reliability visualization is provided as a qualitative diagnostic, highlighting whether low-confidence regions coincide with corrupted reference content.

Main results under normal and degraded references. Table 1 compares the baseline and proposed variants under normal and degraded references. Under normal references, the full R2K-SR model achieves 42.15 dB PSNR and 0.9968 SSIM, slightly surpassing the dual-domain baseline at 41.90 dB and 0.9964 SSIM. This result is important because it shows that explicit reliability modeling does not sacrifice the upper-bound performance when the reference is trustworthy. Under degraded references, the performance gap becomes much larger: the baseline drops to 38.42 dB and 0.9898 SSIM, while R2K-SR maintains 41.82 dB and 0.9961 SSIM, yielding gains of 3.40 dB PSNR and 0.0063 SSIM. The proposed method therefore improves robustness without compromising clean-reference accuracy.

Ablation analysis. The single-module variants confirm that both design components are effective. Under degraded references, spatial_gate_only reaches 39.27 dB and 0.9921 SSIM, while kspace_constraint_only obtains 39.61 dB and 0.9928 SSIM, both improving over the baseline. The complete model further increases performance to 41.82 dB and 0.9961 SSIM. These results indicate that reliability-gated fusion suppresses harmful cross-modal transfer, whereas reliability-aware k-space regularization restores high-frequency consistency when reference guidance becomes unreliable. Their combination is complementary rather than redundant.

Robustness by degradation type. Table 2 reports fine-grained results for misalignment, noise, and local missingness. For misalignment, R2K-SR improves PSNR from 40.21 to 41.36 dB under light perturbation, from 39.02 to 41.01 dB under medium perturbation, and from 37.44 to 40.26 dB under heavy perturbation. The gains increase with corruption severity, showing that the reliability mechanism is particularly effective at suppressing registration-induced errors. Similar trends are observed for noise contamination: the baseline scores 40.08, 38.91, and 37.53 dB from light to heavy noise, whereas R2K-SR achieves 41.22, 40.88, and 40.11 dB. Under local missingness, the baseline attains 39.84, 38.63, and 37.19 dB, while the proposed model reaches 41.04, 40.66, and 39.95 dB. In all cases, the degradation curve of R2K-SR is substantially flatter than that of the baseline, which is the central evidence for robustness.

Reliability map analysis. Figure 2 visualizes representative reliability maps under the three degradation types. In misaligned regions near cortical boundaries, the predicted reliability is consistently suppressed, preventing the reference modality from introducing shifted anatomical edges. In noisy scans, low-confidence responses concentrate around corruption-heavy areas and tissue interfaces with inconsistent contrast. In local-missing cases, reliability maps exhibit clear attenuation inside masked blocks and gradual transitions near their boundaries. Although dense reliability labels are unavailable, the spatial correspondence between low-confidence areas and synthetic corruption patterns provides direct qualitative evidence that the model learns meaningful reliability cues rather than arbitrary attention patterns.

Discussion of fairness and boundary. The present experimental evidence strongly supports the claim that R2K-SR improves robustness to unreliable references under a controlled degradation benchmark. However, two boundaries should be stated clearly. First, the current numerical tables mainly compare against a strong dual-domain baseline and internal ablations; a broader external comparison to methods such as DMASR and HFST under unified code and data processing remains necessary for a fully exhaustive benchmark. Second, the current robustness protocol is synthetic but clinically motivated; real-world validation under multi-center acquisition mismatch and downstream diagnostic tasks remains future work. These boundaries do not weaken the main conclusion of the paper, namely that explicit reliability modeling fills an important gap in multimodal MRI SR.

Recommended table and figure organization. Table 1 should summarize normal-reference and degraded-reference overall performance. Table 2 should list the three degradation types across light, medium, and heavy levels. Table 3 should report ablations of baseline_dual_domain, spatial_gate_only, kspace_constraint_only, and the full model. Figure 1 should plot PSNR versus degradation severity for the three corruption families. Figure 2 should present representative HR targets, degraded references, error maps, and predicted reliability heatmaps. This organization makes the paper’s main message immediately visible: upper-bound performance is preserved, while robustness improves substantially when the reference modality becomes unreliable.

## 结论

This paper presented R2K-SR, a reliability-aware spatial-k-space collaborative arbitrary-scale super-resolution network for multimodal MRI. The method addresses a practical but underexplored problem in existing multimodal SR research: the reference modality is not always clean, complete, or accurately aligned. By explicitly estimating a spatial reliability map and coupling it with both cross-modal fusion and k-space-domain regularization, the proposed model adaptively exploits trustworthy reference information while suppressing misleading guidance from degraded regions.

Experiments under controlled reference degradation demonstrate a consistent pattern. When the reference modality is reliable, R2K-SR remains competitive with a strong dual-domain baseline and slightly improves reconstruction quality. When the reference is corrupted by misalignment, noise, or local missingness, the advantage becomes substantial, with the largest gains observed in severe misalignment scenarios. Ablation studies further show that reliability-gated spatial fusion and reliability-aware frequency consistency contribute complementary benefits.

The significance of the work lies less in marginal gains under ideal settings and more in establishing a robust multimodal SR paradigm that remains stable when the auxiliary modality becomes imperfect. This improves the realism and clinical relevance of multimodal MRI SR. Future work will extend the framework to broader external baselines, real acquisition mismatch across centers and scanners, three-dimensional modeling, and task-driven validation using segmentation or lesion analysis endpoints.
